## Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly equipped with safety alignment mechanisms, yet recent studies demonstrate that they remain vulnerable to jailbreaking attacks that elicit harmful be-haviors without explicit policy violations. While a growing body of work has explored automated jailbreak strategies, existing methods face several fundamental challenges, including the lack of system-atic utilization of both successful and failed attack experiences, as well as the absence of principled mechanisms for composing and selecting reusable attack rules under diverse constraints. As a result, existing methods struggle to accumulate transferable knowledge over time and to reliably adapt attack strategies across different targets and evolving safety mechanisms. To address these issues, we propose a Self-Evolving Rule-Driven Training-Free Jailbreak (SRTJ ) framework that systematically discovers, composes, and refines attack strategies through interaction and feedback, without updating model parameters. Specifically, SRTJ couples experience-driven attack generation with answer set programming (ASP)-based rule selection and constraint-aware composition, where iterative verifier feedback is leveraged to jointly refine successful strategies and analyze failure patterns. The resulting rule memory evolves in a hierarchical multi-level manner, explicitly organizing distilled attack knowledge into long-term, middle-term, and short-term rules, thereby capturing both stable transferable strategies and transient adaptive behaviors to effectively balance exploration and exploitation across attack attempts. Extensive experiments on main-stream jailbreak benchmark (HarmBench) demonstrate that SRTJ 

achieves strong and stable attack performance across different tar-get LLMs, while exhibiting improved robustness and generalization compared to existing jailbreak methods. The code is available at https://github.com/TheSolkatt/SRTJ. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) [ 1, 2, 11 , 17 , 18 , 38 ] have become a core component of modern artificial intelligence, demonstrating strong performance across a wide range of tasks such as natural language understanding, reasoning, code generation, and question answering. Owing to large-scale pretraining and instruction tuning, LLMs are now widely deployed in both consumer-facing applica-tions and high-stakes domains, serving as versatile foundations for building intelligent systems. Despite their impressive performance, LLMs also raise serious safety and alignment concerns [ 16 , 44 , 45 ]. When exposed to ma-licious or adversarial inputs, aligned models may still generate harmful or policy-violating content, including instructions for ille-gal activities, privacy breaches, or other misuse. To mitigate these risks, modern LLMs are typically equipped with safety mechanisms such as reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), safety fine-tuning, and rule-based refusal policies. The study of LLM jailbreaking [ 8, 26 , 40 , 45 ] has emerged as an important research topic for systematically exposing vulnerabilities of aligned language models under adversarial prompting, while also serving as a practical tool for stress-testing and improving safety mechanisms. Existing approaches mainly focus on attack prompt generation and optimization, ranging from manually crafted templates and role-playing strategies to automated, model-in-the-loop methods that iteratively refine attacks based on target feedback, including optimization-based attacks such as GCG [ 51 ], adaptive prompt attacks such as AutoDAN [ 24 ], and agent-based frameworks emphasizing automation and scalability. Despite notable progress, existing jailbreak methods still face fundamental challenges in effectively accumulating and exploiting attack knowledge. (i) Lack of efficient and systematic utiliza-tion of both successful and failed attack experiences. While some prior work incorporates memory or history mechanisms, these experiences are often stored in a naive or unstructured man-ner, without distilling concise and reusable attack rules, leading to inefficient reuse and limited long-term benefit. (ii) Absence of principled mechanisms for composing and selecting reusable attack rules under diverse constraints. Existing methods largely rely on implicit prompt heuristics or ad hoc strategies, lacking explicit and controllable procedures for selecting and composing reusable attack rules that can adapt to varying attack goals, con-straints, and target models. As a result, existing methods struggle to accumulate transferable knowledge over time and to reliably adapt attack strategies across different targets and evolving safety mechanisms.

In this work, we propose SRTJ , a self-evolving, rule-driven, and training-free jailbreak framework that systematically accumulates, abstracts, and exploits attack experience over time. Instead of treat-ing jailbreak prompts as isolated artifacts, SRTJ explicitly models jailbreak as an experience-driven process, where successful attack behaviors are progressively distilled into reusable symbolic rules. To tackle challenge (i) , SRTJ introduces a hierarchical, multi-level memory to organize accumulated jailbreak experience. Rather than storing raw attack prompts in a naive manner, SRTJ extracts and consolidates successful attack patterns into concise and gen-eralizable rules, which are selectively retained based on their ef-fectiveness and success frequency. This design enables efficient long-term reuse of high-quality attack strategies while preventing uncontrolled memory growth and noise accumulation. To overcome challenge (ii) , SRTJ leverages answer set program-ming (ASP) as a symbolic reasoning engine to perform explicit, constraint-aware rule selection and composition. Given an attack goal and contextual constraints, ASP systematically identifies com-patible rules and assembles them into valid attack strategies, of-fering stronger controllability and compositional reliability than purely language-based reasoning. By decoupling symbolic strategy planning from natural language realization, SRTJ remains robust to prompt surface variations and adaptable to diverse target LLMs. During deployment, SRTJ iteratively refines its rule repository based on accumulated attack outcomes, enabling progressively more effective rule composition without any model training. This process leads to strong and stable attack performance across dif-ferent target LLMs, as illustrated in Fig. 1 for both single-turn and multi-turn settings. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: 

• We propose SRTJ , a novel training-free jailbreak framework that formulates LLM jailbreaking as a self-evolving, rule-driven process, enabling systematic discovery and reuse of attack strategies without model fine-tuning. 

• We introduce an experience accumulation and rule evolution mechanism that integrates symbolic rule composition with LLM-based attack generation, allowing effective knowledge consolidation across jailbreak attempts. 

• We conduct extensive experiments on the mainstream jail-break benchmark (HarmBench) and target LLMs, demon-strating that SRTJ achieves strong and stable attack perfor-mance with improved robustness and generalization com-pared to existing methods. 

## 2 Related Work 2.1 LLM Jailbreaking 

2.1.1 Single-turn Attacks. Single-turn methods aim to bypass the safety alignment of large language models (LLMs) using a single prompt, without iterative interaction or multi-round feed-back. These methods typically operate at the prompt level and ex-ploit weaknesses in safety generalization through one-shot or non-interactive attacks. A line of work focuses on adversarial prompt optimization. GCG [ 51 ] proposes a greedy coordinate gradient method to construct universal adversarial suffixes that induce harm-ful responses when appended to benign queries. PAIR [ 6 ] studies a black-box setting where an attacker LLM automatically refines prompts based on model feedback, showing that effective jailbreak prompts can be discovered with a small number of queries. Other ap-proaches emphasize semantic and natural-language-based prompt manipulation. AutoDAN [ 24 ] uses a hierarchical genetic algorithm to generate fluent and semantically meaningful jailbreak prompts, demonstrating improved stealthiness against perplexity-based de-fenses. PAP [ 47 ] investigates persuasion-based attacks, showing that human-like conversational framing can elicit unsafe responses from aligned models. Several works explore representation shifts that expose safety generalization gaps. Multilingual jailbreaks [ 9] re-veal that safety alignment trained on high-resource languages may not generalize well to other languages. CipherChat [ 46 ] encodes inputs using ciphers, leading to increased harmful behaviors under encoded prompts. CodeAttack [ 31 ] reformulates harmful queries as code completion tasks, demonstrating that safety mechanisms trained on natural language may fail to generalize to code-based inputs. ReNeLLM [ 10 ] further shows that nested and compositional prompt structures can induce jailbreaks within a single forward pass. 

2.1.2 Multi-turn Attacks. Multi-turn jailbreaking attacks exploit the conversational nature of large language models (LLMs) by dis-tributing malicious intent across multiple interaction rounds, rather than issuing a single explicit harmful query. By leveraging dia-logue history, contextual accumulation, and intermediate model responses, these methods aim to bypass safety mechanisms that are primarily optimized for single-turn inputs. A line of work adopts role-playing or hypothetical scenarios to guide models toward un-safe behaviors over multiple turns. Red Queen [ 19 ] shows that LLMs can be induced to generate harmful outputs when prompts are framed as fictional settings or assigned roles (e.g., “You are a role . . . ”), revealing latent multi-turn safety risks that do not manifest under isolated queries. Similarly, CFA [ 37 ] studies multi-turn semantic jailbreaks and proposes a context fusion strategy that constructs contextual scenarios around a target query, dynam-ically integrating and replacing malicious keywords to conceal direct harmful intent while preserving semantic relevance. Other approaches rely on fixed communication patterns or progressive escalation strategies. Crescendo [ 33 ] facilitates benign interactions across multiple turns and incrementally steers the model toward un-safe behaviors by referencing its own previous responses. CoA [ 41 ]frames multi-turn jailbreaking as an interrogation process, adap-tively generating attack chains based on dialogue history and se-mantic feedback to gradually expose harmful capabilities. More re-cently, ActorBreaker [ 32 ] investigates multi-turn jailbreaks through natural distribution shifts, constructing attack trajectories that ex-ploit semantic relationships between benign and toxic concepts. By modeling interactions among different actors in a dialogue, Actor-Breaker demonstrates that aligned LLMs may exhibit safety gaps under naturally evolving multi-turn conversations.

## 3 Methodology 

In this section, we present SRTJ , a self-evolving, rule-driven, and training-free framework for LLM jailbreaking. Unlike prior ap-proaches that rely on handcrafted prompt templates or model fine-tuning, SRTJ explicitly models jailbreak attacks as a structured, se-quential process that accumulates experience and evolves reusable strategies over time. The core design principle of SRTJ is to de-couple symbolic decision making from natural language realization ,enabling systematic strategy search under black-box access and limited query budgets. An overview of our framework is illustrated in Fig. 2. The end-to-end execution of SRTJ is formally specified in Algorithm 1, which details the iterative interaction among at-tack generation, verifier feedback, and experience evolution. (More Details are in Appendix B)

## 3.3 Rule Representation and Symbolic Abstraction 

A central component of SRTJ is the use of symbolic rules to repre-sent reusable jailbreak experience. Each rule 𝜌 ∈ R is defined as a structured abstraction 

𝜌 = (𝑢, Φ, z, 𝜔 ), (8) where 𝑢 is a natural language description of the underlying strategy, 

Φ = {𝜙 𝑘 } is a set of symbolic predicates, z denotes associated se-mantic tags, and 𝜔 stores statistical attributes such as usage counts and success rates. The predicate set Φ is drawn from a predefined ontology and cap-tures high-level strategy properties, including persuasion strategy, linguistic tone, formatting style, and structural constraints. By de-sign, predicates are domain-agnostic and don’t encode goal-specific operational details. This abstraction enables rules to be reused across different goals while remaining compatible with symbolic reasoning. Detailed roles of symbolic reasoning are in Appendix B.1. Crucially, rules represent strategic intent rather than concrete prompt realizations. This separation allows SRTJ to reason over strategies at a symbolic level while delegating surface-level lan-guage generation to a separate instantiation module. 

## 3.4 Rule-Guided Attack Generation via Answer Set Programming (ASP) 

Attack generation in SRTJ proceeds through a two-stage process that decouples symbolic strategy selection from natural language realization, where ASP enables constraint-aware strategy selection over evolving jailbreak rules. Definition and more details of ASP are in Appendix B.2. 

Exploratory generation. When the rule library is empty or in-sufficient, i.e., R𝑡 = ∅, the system performs exploratory attack gen-eration. In this mode, prompts are generated directly by a language model to probe the target model’s safety mechanisms. Exploratory attempts are strictly bounded to control query cost and serve as the primary source of initial experience for rule discovery. This bounded exploratory attack loop, which is used for cold-start ex-ploration and as a fallback when rule-guided generation becomes ineffective, is formally specified in Algorithm 2 (Appendix B.3). 

Rule-guided generation. Once reusable rules have been accu-mulated, SRTJ switches to rule-guided attack generation. Given the current rule library R𝑡 , accumulated experience E𝑡 −1, and goal 

𝑔 , the system solves a symbolic rule selection problem: 

𝜋 𝑡 ∈ ASP-Solve (R 𝑡 , 𝑔, E𝑡 −1), (9) where 𝜋 𝑡 ⊆ R 𝑡 denotes a compatible set of rules. The ASP formulation encodes rule applicability conditions, mu-tual exclusion constraints, coverage requirements, and prohibitions against previously failed rule combinations. By explicitly modeling these constraints, SRTJ can systematically explore the combinato-rial space of constraint-aware strategy compositions while avoiding invalid or redundant attempts. After selecting a rule set 𝜋 𝑡 , a language model instantiates the symbolic strategies into a concrete prompt 

𝑝 𝑡 ∼ G( 𝑔, 𝜋 𝑡 ), (10)

## 3.5 Verifier-Guided Feedback Signal 

The verifier plays a critical role in enabling self-evolution with-out model training. Our design is conceptually inspired by recent training-free optimization paradigms, particularly Training-Free GRPO [ 5 , 34 ], which leverage external feedback to guide behavior without updating model parameters. Unlike GRPO-style methods that perform policy optimization, the verifier in SRTJ serves purely as an internal feedback oracle, providing signals for experience accumulation and rule evolution rather than model learning. For each response 𝑟 𝑡 , the verifier returns a scalar score 𝑠 𝑡 and, when applicable, a textual failure analysis 𝑓 𝑡 . The binary success indicator 𝑦 𝑡 = I(𝑠 𝑡 ≥ 𝜏 ) provides a unified supervision signal for downstream modules. Unlike external evaluation metrics, the verifier is used exclu-sively as an internal feedback oracle. Its outputs guide experience accumulation, rule harvesting, and credit assignment, but don’t directly determine final performance reporting. Failure analyses are retained and incorporated into subsequent attack generation to discourage repeated failure while preserving symbolic constraints.

## 3.7 Overall Algorithmic Flow 

Overall, SRTJ operates as a closed-loop, self-evolving system. For each goal 𝑔 , the framework alternates between symbolic rule selec-tion, prompt instantiation, verifier-guided feedback, and experience-driven rule evolution. Over time, the rule library expands and re-fines, enabling progressively more effective and transferable jail-break attacks under black-box access and limited query budgets, without relying on model training or gradient-based optimization.

Why symbolic rules instead of prompt templates? While prompt templates provide a convenient way to express attacks, they are inherently tied to surface-level linguistic realizations and tend to generalize poorly across goals and contexts. SRTJ instead adopts symbolic rules as a higher-level abstraction of jailbreak experience, capturing reusable strategy patterns that are decoupled from spe-cific prompt phrasings. This abstraction supports principled rule composition, systematic exploration under constraints, and efficient reuse of distilled attack knowledge, avoiding repeated rediscovery of similar behaviors. 

Why verifier-based feedback? Rather than relying on hand-crafted heuristics or direct prompt optimization, SRTJ employs a verifier as an internal feedback signal to guide experience accu-mulation and rule evolution. The verifier provides coarse-grained success indicators and auxiliary failure information that inform subsequent attack generation, without performing model learn-ing or serving as a final evaluation metric. This design enables iterative refinement of attack strategies while preserving a training-free paradigm and maintaining a clear separation between attack generation and evaluation.

## 4.4 Hyper-parameter Analysis 

We analyze the impact of the hyper-parameter round , which con-trols the number of experience harvesting and rule evolution it-erations in SRTJ . Each round consists of attack generation, ex-perience collection, rule abstraction, and memory update. This hyper-parameter directly reflects the efficiency of the proposed evolving memory mechanism, as larger values correspond to higher interaction budgets; numerical results are reported in Table 5 (Ap-pendix C.1). Fig. 4 (b) shows that the attack success rate generally improves as the number of rounds increases from 1 to 5, with clear saturation after a few iterations. For GPT-3.5, GPT-4o, and LLaMA-3-70B, performance improves rapidly in early rounds and stabilizes around rounds 4–5, indicating efficient accumulation of high-quality rules. In contrast, LLaMA-3-8B exhibits slower and less stable early improvements, followed by a notable performance jump at round 4, suggesting that smaller models require more accumu-lated experience before effective rule compositions emerge. Across all models, increasing the number of rounds beyond 4 yields di-minishing returns, demonstrating that SRTJ achieves strong attack performance with a limited and controllable interaction budget.

## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we proposed SRTJ , a self-evolving, rule-driven, and training-free framework for LLM jailbreaking. By reframing jail-break attacks as a process of efficient experience utilization and principled rule composition, SRTJ enables systematic discovery, abstraction, and reuse of effective attack strategies without relying on model training or handcrafted prompt heuristics. Leveraging concise multi-level experiences distilled from successful attacks and constraint-aware rule selection via answer set programming (ASP), SRTJ demonstrates strong and stable attack performance on the HarmBench benchmark across both proprietary and open-source target LLMs. More broadly, our findings suggest that elevat-ing jailbreaking from prompt-level optimization to strategy-level experience evolution offers a promising direction for scalable, trans-ferable, and interpretable evaluation of LLM safety mechanisms.

## A Additional Related Work A.1 Formalization or Logic-Based Approaches 

Recent surveys have highlighted the growing role of logical rea-soning and neuro-symbolic frameworks in improving the reason-ing robustness, controllability, and safety of large language mod-els [ 4, 7, 23 , 42 ]. These studies systematically review how formal logic, symbolic representations, and rule-based reasoning can com-plement neural language models and expose limitations of purely data-driven approaches. Building on this line of work, several re-cent studies have explored symbolic and logic-based formulations in the context of LLM safety and jailbreaking. LogiBreak [ 30 ] for-mulates malicious intents using formal logical expressions and exploits logical equivalence and transformation rules to bypass safety constraints, demonstrating that aligned models remain vul-nerable to semantically equivalent but structurally transformed logical inputs. MathPrompt [ 3 ] encodes harmful prompts into sym-bolic mathematics problems using set theory, abstract algebra, and symbolic logic, revealing that safety mechanisms trained on natu-ral language inputs fail to generalize to mathematically encoded representations. Beyond jailbreak-specific settings, symbolic rea-soning has also been explored through rule-based representations. RuleGenie [ 36 ] leverages large language models to analyze, com-pare, and optimize symbolic rule sets under explicit logical and performance constraints, illustrating how LLMs can be integrated with structured rule systems for reasoning over complex symbolic programs. Overall, existing jailbreak methods mainly focus on prompt-level manipulation or interaction strategies under specific attack settings. As a result, attack attempts are often treated as isolated processes, and successful experiences are rarely abstracted into reusable and transferable knowledge. In parallel, prior research on LLM memory primarily targets general agent behavior, long-horizon interaction, or self-improvement, with limited exploration in adversarial attack generation. These limitations suggest the need for mechanisms that can systematically accumulate, organize, and exploit attack experience over time. 

## B Additional Methodology B.1 Symbolic Reasoning 

Symbolic reasoning provides a formal mechanism for representing structured knowledge and enforcing logical constraints through explicit rules. In contrast to purely neural approaches that rely on implicit pattern learning, symbolic methods enable interpretable, compositional, and constraint-aware reasoning, which is particu-larly suitable for tasks involving rule selection, compatibility check-ing, and strategy composition. Specifically, this work utilizes the Answer Set Programming (ASP) system and symbolic reasoning to abstract and organize jailbreak strategies at the rule level, enabling structured reuse and controlled composition across different attack goals and categories. 

B.1.1 Answer Set Programming (ASP) . Answer Set Program-ming (ASP) is a declarative logic programming paradigm designed for solving combinatorial search and constraint satisfaction prob-lems. ASP programs consist of a set of logical rules and constraints, whose computed solutions, named answer sets , represent stable and self-consistent models that satisfy all specified conditions. An ASP program is typically composed of several basic syntactic elements: Atoms are the basis of the ASP program and specify the most fundamental semantic units, representing the propositions that can be either true or false; Facts specify unconditional truths; 

Rules define logical dependencies between atoms and enable the derivation of new conclusions under specified conditions; Con-straints are used to prohibit undesired combinations of atoms and encode compatibility or applicability restrictions; Choice rules allow the ASP to select arbitrary subsets of atoms from a given domain, supporting flexible combination and selection behaviors. Formally, the ASP program is defined over a set of atoms and typically includes rules of the form: 

𝑎 :- 𝑏 1, . . . , 𝑏 𝑚 , not 𝑐 1, . . . , not 𝑐 𝑛 , (14) where 𝑎 , 𝑏 𝑖 , and 𝑐 𝑗 are atoms, and not denotes default negation. Constraints can be expressed by the above rules with empty heads, which eliminate invalid combinations of atoms from admissible answer sets. In the context of our SRTJ , ASP serves as a symbolic abstrac-tion mechanism for rule composition. High-level jailbreak rules are treated as symbolic units, and ASP-style constraints are used to enforce compatibility, applicability, and structural consistency when composing multiple rules into a unified attack strategy. By decoupling symbolic strategy selection from surface-level prompt realization, this symbolic formulation facilitates modular reuse of jailbreak knowledge while preserving flexibility in natural lan-guage generation. Moreover, the non-monotonic nature of ASP offers a conceptual foundation for modeling the evolving validity of jailbreak rules where newly accumulated experience or failure feedback may invalidate previously effective strategy combinations.

## C.5 Ethical Statement 

This work aims to advance the understanding and evaluation of safety vulnerabilities in large language models (LLMs) with the ultimate goal of improving their robustness and alignment. Our study is conducted in the context of responsible AI research and is motivated by the growing need for systematic red-teaming method-ologies that can expose failure modes of safety mechanisms under realistic black-box settings. All experiments are performed on established benchmarks following prior work in automated red-teaming and LLM safety assessment. Case studies included in this paper are partially redacted and intentionally presented at a high level to illustrate risks without enabling replication. This work does not involve human subjects, personal data, or sensitive user information, and therefore does not require institu-tional review board (IRB) approval. We hope that our findings can help the research community and practitioners better understand the limitations of current safety mechanisms and contribute to the development of more robust, transparent, and trustworthy LLM systems.
