Abstract 

Multi-turn jailbreaks exploit the ability of large language models to accumulate and act on conversational context. Instead of stating a harmful request directly, an attacker can gradually steer the conversation toward an unsafe answer. Recent methods demonstrate this risk, but they are usually evaluated as black-box pipelines with different budgets, judges, retry rules, and strategy generation procedures. As a result, it is often unclear whether reported gains reflect stronger attack mechanisms or different experimental conditions. We introduce MT-JailBench, a modular evaluation framework for benchmarking multi-turn jailbreaks under fixed conditions. MT-JailBench implements each attack as five interacting modules: evaluation function, attack strategy, prompt generation, prompt refinement, and flow control. This design enables fair comparison across attack methods and component-wise analysis of what drives attack success. Using MT-JailBench, we find that resource budgets and evaluation functions are major confounders: controlling turns, retries, interactions, sampled strategies, and judges substantially change the ranking of attacks. At the component level, prompt generation accounts for most performance variation, while refinement and flow control provide moderate gains. We also find that explicit dynamic strategy generation is not always necessary; stochastic sampling from a fixed strategy can rival more elaborate diversification mechanisms. Finally, recomposing the best components yields a strong attack configuration that outperforms its source attacks and generalizes across diverse target LLMs. MT-JailBench therefore provides a modular framework for comparing multi-turn jailbreaks, understanding the impact of components, and guiding stronger red-teaming evaluations.

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used as conversational agents that remember prior context, adapt to user feedback, and help refine objectives over multiple turns [ 1, 7]. This ability makes them useful for decomposing difficult problems, revising instructions, and supporting multi-step reasoning [ 50 ] or iterative planning [ 25 , 49 ]. However, it also expands the attack surface. Although harmful intent in a single-turn attack is usually localized within one prompt [ 40 , 36 ], multi-turn jailbreaks 1

exploit the conversational structure itself [ 21 , 43 ]. Rather than searching for a single prompt that elicits harmful content, the attacker can distribute the objective across the interaction: each prompt may appear benign, while the accumulated context steers the model toward an unsafe response. Early turns can establish a benign context, encourage the model to adopt a cooperative stance, and introduce assumptions that appear harmless before the harmful objective becomes explicit. By the time the request is sharpened, the model may have accepted parts of the framing or produced intermediate context that makes refusal less likely. This trajectory matters because many safety mechanisms are most reliable when harmful intent is explicit and localized to a single turn, and are less suited to objectives assembled gradually over a long context [ 10 ]. 20

Recent multi-turn attacks instantiate this pattern in differ-ent ways: Crescendo gradually steers benign conversations toward harmful outputs through context accumulation [ 29 ]; Chain of Attack dynamically refines the interaction as a semantic progression [ 39 ]; Foot-In-The-Door exploits incre-mental compliance over successive turns [ 37 ]; and X-Teaming uses multiple agents to plan, execute, and optimize attack trajectories [ 26 ]. These attacks matter not because they are merely longer versions of single-turn jailbreaks, but because they exploit the same mechanism that makes conversational LLMs useful: the ability to accumulate context, infer intent, and act on it. Despite the growing safety concern posed by multi-turn attacks, there has been little systematic study of how existing methods compare when surrounding conditions are held fixed [22]. As a result, it often remains unclear what makes these attacks work and which failure modes defenses should target. Most attacks are introduced as complete pipelines, each with its own budget, judge, retry rule, and strategy generation procedure. One method may be allowed more turns, another may restart from many sampled strategies, and another may use a more permissive judge. Thus, the central issue is not only which method achieves the highest score, but why it does so [ 32 ]. We therefore need a benchmark that places existing methods in a common experimental setting and decomposes them into parts so that we can identify which components are responsible for improved performance. This motivates our research question: When multi-turn jailbreaks are evaluated under fixed experimental conditions, which components drive their success? To study this question, we introduce MT-JailBench, an evaluation framework for benchmarking multi-turn jailbreaks under fixed experimental conditions. Rather than treating each attack as a black-box pipeline, MT-JailBench implements multi-turn jailbreaks as five interacting modules: evaluation function, attack strategy, prompt generation, prompt refinement, and flow control. The framework supports fixed external conditions, including the number of turns, retries, sampled strategies, and evaluation criteria, while its modular design enables controlled variation of individual modules to measure their contribution to attack success rate (ASR). Our study yields five main findings: 

• Resource-controlled comparison. We compare five existing attacks after fixing turns, retries, target interactions, and sampled strategies. We find that resource budgets substantially affect reported ASR. Some methods lose much of their advantage under constrained search budgets, showing that ASR partly measures how much search the attacker was allowed to perform. 2• Judge-controlled evaluation. We evaluate the same attack outputs with different judges under a shared protocol. We find that measured success changes substantially with the evaluation function because judges draw different boundaries between failure, partial progress, and success. 

• Component-wise analysis. After controlling budgets and judges, we ablate prompt generation, refinement, and flow control one module at a time. We find that prompt generation accounts for most performance variation, while refinement and flow control provide moderate gains. 

• Strategy diversification analysis. We compare explicit strategy enumeration against repeated stochastic sampling from a fixed strategy, with refinement and flow control held fixed. We find that stochastic sampling can rival more elaborate diversification mechanisms when paired with a strong generator and effective flow control. This suggests that progress in multi-turn red teaming depends not only on expanding the attack space, but also on understanding how that space is searched. 

• Component recomposition. We recombine strong components from Crescendo and XTeaming into CrescendoX . We find that across open-weight and proprietary target models, CrescendoX 

outperforms its source attacks on 19 of 21 target LLMs; Figure 1 shows selected results. This shows that MT-JailBench can be used not only to compare attacks, but also to identify reusable components and guide stronger multi-turn red-teaming methods. Beyond these findings, MT-JailBench is designed as a reusable evaluation framework rather than a closed comparison among a fixed set of attacks. Its modular interface makes it easy to add new prompt generators, refinement methods, judges, control policies, target models, and datasets. Although our experiments focus on attacks, the framework also supports incorporating defenses such as model guardrails, input transformations, and output filters. MT-JailBench can therefore evaluate new multi-turn jailbreak methods and test whether defenses remain effective when the attacker adapts over several turns. More broadly, it provides a common experimental language for multi-turn red teaming, where progress can be measured not only by higher attack success rates, but also by a clearer account of which assumptions, budgets, and evaluation choices support those rates. 

Contributions. Our main contributions are as follows. 

• A modular evaluation framework for benchmarking multi-turn jailbreaks. We introduce MT-JailBench, which implements multi-turn jailbreaks as five interacting modules: evaluation function, attack strategy, prompt generation, prompt refinement, and flow control. This design provides a reusable framework for evaluating and analyzing existing and future methods. 

• A controlled empirical study of evaluation confounders. Using MT-JailBench, we show that resource budgets and evaluation functions substantially affect reported attack success. After controlling for these factors, the prompt generation mechanism accounts for most performance variation, while prompt refinement and flow control provide moderate gains. 

• Benchmark-guided recomposition of stronger attacks. We combine the best-performing components identified by MT-JailBench and evaluate the resulting configuration across target LLMs. This shows that MT-JailBench can be used not only to compare attacks, but also to guide the design of stronger multi-turn red-teaming methods and support future evaluation of defenses. 

# 2 Problem Setup 

We study multi-turn jailbreaks in a black-box setting [ 21 ]. Let M denote a target LLM, and let x ∈ X 

denote a harmful behavior that the attacker wants the model to produce. The attacker has no access to the model parameters, logits, safety classifiers, hidden states, or system prompt. Instead, the attacker interacts with M only through natural language queries and observes only the model’s textual responses. A multi-turn jailbreak is a sequential interaction between the attacker and the target model. At turn 3t, the attacker sends a prompt qt, and the target model produces a response 

rt = M(qt | Ht−1), (1) where Ht−1 = {(qi, r i)}t−1 

> i=1

denotes the conversation history before turn t. After the response is generated, the history is updated as Ht = Ht−1 ∪ { (qt, r t)}. An attack therefore produces a prompt sequence 

Q = ( q1, q 2, . . . , q T ) and a corresponding response sequence R = ( r1, r 2, . . . , r T ). The attacker’s goal is not necessarily to make every intermediate response harmful [ 26 ]. In many multi-turn attacks, early turns are used to establish context, obtain related information, introduce a benign framing, or move the model into a cooperative conversational state [ 43 ]. The harmful objective may only become clear after several turns, once the conversation has accumulated enough context. We evaluate this outcome with an evaluation function E, which maps either a response or a conversation history together with the harmful target to an attack-success signal: 

et = E(rt, x ) or et = E(Ht, x ).

Depending on the evaluator, et may be a binary label, a refusal indicator, a similarity score, or a graded harmfulness score. In the simplest case, E is applied only to the final response to determine whether the attack succeeded. In adaptive multi-turn attacks, however, evaluation signals can also be used to guide later interactions. This distinction matters because adaptive multi-turn jailbreaks differ not only in the prompts they generate, but also in how they use feedback from previous turns. 

# 3 MT-JailBench 

Multi-turn jailbreaks are harder to analyze than single-turn jailbreaks because the attack is no longer defined by one prompt and one response [ 22 ]. In the single-turn case, most of the attack logic is contained in the prompt itself [ 24 ]. In the multi-turn case, success depends on a sequence of decisions [ 21 ]: what prompt to send, how to use the model’s previous responses, when to retry, when to change direction, and when to stop. It also depends on external choices such as the number of allowed turns and sampled strategies, as well as the evaluator used to decide whether the attack succeeded. Existing multi-turn jailbreak methods usually package all of these choices into complete attack pipelines. This makes them easy to execute but difficult to understand. When one method achieves a higher attack success rate than another, the improvement may come from better prompt generation, a more effective control policy, or simply a larger search budget. Without separating these factors, it is difficult to know which parts of the attack are actually responsible for the success. 

## 3.1 Modular Evaluation Framework 

MT-JailBench is designed to evaluate multi-turn jailbreaks under fixed experimental conditions while allowing the internal attack design to vary. A benchmark instance consists of a harmful behavior x, a target model M, an attacker configuration, an evaluation function, and a resource budget. The resource budget specifies the maximum number of turns, retries, target-model interactions, and sampled attack strategies. By fixing these quantities, MT-JailBench separates the strength of an attack mechanism from the amount of search the attacker is allowed to perform. Figure 2 provides a conceptual overview of MT-JailBench. Rather than treating an attack as a single end-to-end script, MT-JailBench represents each multi-turn jailbreak as five interacting components:

This decomposition is not only descriptive. In MT-JailBench, attack components are interchangeable modules, allowing attacks to run end to end or be modified one component at a time while the rest of the configuration remains fixed. As a result, the framework supports attack-level comparison, component-level analysis, and recomposition into new attack configurations. In implementation, MT-JailBench operationalizes the five functional components through three standardized interfaces: PromptGenerator ,

OutcomeEvaluator , and FlowController . The PromptGenerator produces attack prompts and performs prompt refinement when requested by the control policy. The OutcomeEvaluator determines whether the current response satisfies the attack objective and may return auxiliary signals such as harmfulness scores, refusal indicators, or similarity scores. The FlowController governs the attack trajectory by deciding whether to advance, retry, restart, or terminate. The framework handles the remaining execution logic, including target-model interaction, conversation-state tracking, resource accounting, and coordination across modules. We provide the full system design, including concrete programming interfaces and workflow orchestration, in Appendix C. 

## 3.2 Multi-Turn Attack Decomposition 

We now describe how the five modules are executed during an attack. At turn t, the prompt generator produces a prompt, the target model responds conditioned on the conversation history, the evaluator scores the response with respect to the harmful target, and the flow controller selects the next action: 

qt ∼ G (π, x, H t−1, e <t ; γ), rt = M(qt | Ht−1), et = E(rt, x ), at = C(Ht, e t). (3) Here Ht = {(qi, r i)}ti=1 is the conversation history, et the evaluation signal, and at the control action selected after turn t. Depending on at, MT-JailBench may advance, retry, restart, or terminate the attack. This loop defines the common execution interface used by all attacks; methods differ in how they instantiate these modules. We next describe the role of each component in the framework. 1. Attack strategy. The attack strategy π provides high-level guidance for the conversation before any particular prompt is sent. It may specify a benign framing, a persona, a sequence of topics, or a gradual path from background information to a more specific request. The prompt generation module G turns this guidance into the concrete prompts. Separating strategy from generation lets MT-JailBench distinguish between selecting a better conversational path and writing better prompts along that path. The strategy may be fixed, for example by using few-shot examples, or generated dynamically using an LLM, for example by prompting for diversified personas. 2. Prompt generation. The prompt generation module G produces the next prompt from the strategy, harmful target, conversation history, and previous evaluation signals. The generation 5mode γ ∈ { offline , online } specifies whether prompts are planned before interaction or generated adaptively during interaction. In the offline setting, the attack first generates a prompt sequence 

Q ∼ G (π, x ) and then follows this sequence during the conversation. In the online setting, each prompt is generated based on the current history and feedback. This distinction separates the quality of a planned trajectory from the ability to react to intermediate target-model responses. 3. Evaluation function. The evaluation function E maps the target model’s response and harmful behavior to an evaluation signal et. This signal may be a binary success label, a refusal indicator, a similarity score, or a graded harmfulness score. Evaluation determines whether an attack is counted as successful, and may also provide feedback for prompt refinement and flow control. 4. Flow control. The flow control policy C decides how the attack proceeds after observing the current history and evaluation signal. Its action at may advance the attack to the next turn, retry the current turn, restart by rolling back to the first or another earlier turn, or terminate the attack. Flow control determines how the attack spends its limited budget: whether to continue along the current trajectory, repair a local failure, or abandon an unpromising path. 5. Prompt refinement. When the flow controller requests retrying the current turn, the refinement module U updates the current prompt using the evaluation signal, qt ← U (qt, e t). Refinement differs from generation because it does not begin a new step in the planned conversation. Instead, it repairs the current prompt while preserving the local attack intent. Thus, refinement provides local adaptation, while flow control decides when such adaptation is worth spending budget on. 

## 3.3 Expressing Existing Attacks in MT-JailBench 

The component interfaces can express existing multi-turn jailbreak methods, as shown in Table 1. This mapping is central to MT-JailBench because existing methods differ along several axes at once. One attack may use a binary judge with offline prompt generation, while another may use a score-based judge with online generation and dynamic strategies. Comparing these methods only as end-to-end pipelines does not reveal which design choices are responsible for performance differences. MT-JailBench instead maps existing attacks into the common execution interface, turning indivisible pipelines into comparable components. The framework can therefore evaluate complete attacks under fixed budgets and evaluation protocols, replace individual modules for ablation, and recompose high-performing components into new configurations. The same interface also supports extension: new judges, prompt generators, refinement mechanisms, flow controllers, target models, datasets, and defenses can be added without changing the overall execution logic.

## 4.1 How sensitive is attack success to resource budgets? 

We first study the role of resource budgets. In multi-turn jailbreaks, a larger budget does more than make an experiment more expensive: it changes how extensively the attacker can search conversational trajectories. Additional turns, retries, target-model interactions, or sampled strategies give the attacker more opportunities to recover from refusals, abandon unpromising trajectories, and explore alternative paths. As a result, attack success reflects not only the quality of the attack mechanism, but also how much exploration the attacker performs. Thus, before comparing attack mechanisms, we need to ask how much of the performance gap remains when this budget is constrained. We compare each method in two settings. The abundant setting configures each attack according to its original literature and allows up to 50 total interactions. The limited setting imposes the same constraints on all methods: at most three retries per turn, no restart upon failure, and at most 10 total interactions. Table 2 shows that methods vary substantially in how strongly they depend on search budget. ActorBreaker, XTeaming, and CoA lose substantial ASR under limited resources, which is consistent with their control logic: ActorBreaker can switch across actors, XTeaming can restart with different strategies, and CoA can repeatedly backtrack when a trajectory has not succeeded. By contrast, Crescendo and FITD are comparatively stable. Thus, for resource-sensitive methods, ASR partly measures how much search the attack was allowed to perform. Multi-turn red-teaming evaluations should therefore report and control budgets explicitly; otherwise, differences in ASR can reflect different amounts of search rather than better attack mechanisms.

## 4.2 How sensitive is attack success to the evaluation function? 

We next study the role of the evaluation function. Multi-turn jailbreaks often do not produce a clean binary outcome: a response may refuse the final request while still providing useful intermediate information, or satisfy part of the harmful objective without making the violation explicit. Since existing attacks use different judges to define success, reported ASR can change simply because the decision boundary between failure, partial progress, and success is drawn differently. To measure this effect, we compare each method’s self-reported ASR in the abundant setting with a cross-validated ASR, where success requires agreement from all three judges: CoA-Binary , FITD-Binary ,and Score-Judge . Table 3 shows that judge choice can substantially change measured success rates. The largest drops occur for methods evaluated with more permissive binary judges, namely FITD and CoA. In comparison, Score-Judge is more stable under cross-validation, especially for Crescendo and XTeaming. This suggests that part of the apparent gap between attacks reflects differences in evaluation criteria rather than attack behavior. Multi-turn red-teaming evaluations should therefore report the judge, control it in direct comparisons, and cross-validate new judges against existing ones.

## 4.5 Is explicit strategy diversification necessary? 

The component ablation raises a further question. Crescendo uses a fixed high-level strategy, yet provides the strongest generator in our study. This makes it unclear whether multi-turn red teaming requires an explicit pool of diversified strategies, or whether comparable diversity can arise from stochastic generation within a fixed strategy. To test this distinction, we compare Crescendo and XTeaming generation while fixing refinement and flow control to XTeaming. For Crescendo, we sample with temperature =1.2 and top_p =0.9, so repeated runs produce different conversational trajectories under the same strategy. For XTeaming, strategies are generated in advance, and each run uses a distinct strategy from this explicit strategy pool. This setup isolates the source of diversity: stochastic trajectory variation for Crescendo versus explicit strategy enumeration for XTeaming. Figure 4 shows that Crescendo reaches high ASR within the first few runs, whereas XTeaming improves more gradually as additional strategies are explored. Despite these different convergence patterns, the two methods eventually reach comparable performance. This suggests that explicit strategy enumeration is not the only way to explore diverse attack trajectories. A fixed strategy, when paired with stochastic decoding, can still cover the attack space effectively. ## 4.6 CrescendoX : Can strong components be recomposed into a stronger attack? 

The preceding experiments identify prompt generation and flow control as the main sources of perfor-mance variation. We next test whether this component-level finding is predictive rather than merely descriptive. If the component analysis captures transferable mechanisms, then recombining the strongest components should yield an attack that outperforms its source attacks. We therefore combine Crescendo prompt generation with XTeaming refinement and flow control, yielding CrescendoX . We compare 

CrescendoX against its two source attacks. Figure 5 shows that the recomposed attack improves substantially. CrescendoX achieves the highest ASR on 19 of 21 target models, including all evaluated open-weight models and most proprietary models. Its ASR exceeds Crescendo’s by more than 20 percentage points on several targets, including Llama-4-Scout, Gemma-4-E4B-IT, Qwen-3.5-Flash, Grok-4.1-Fast, and Nemotron-3-Nano. It also preserves strong performance where XTeaming is already effective, rather than trading off performance across model families. Absolute ASRs should be interpreted as system-level measurements: proprietary results may reflect deployment safeguards as well as base-model behavior, including GPT-5 safety classifiers 10 and reasoning-based monitoring [ 31 ], Gemini-3-Pro prompt filtering [ 12 ], or Claude’s Petri evaluation framework [ 2]. The main conclusion is that component-level analysis can do more than explain existing attacks: it can predict useful recompositions that generalize across target models. 

# 5 Conclusion 

We introduced MT-JailBench, a modular evaluation framework for benchmarking multi-turn jailbreaks under fixed experimental conditions. Rather than only reporting which attack achieves the highest success rate, MT-JailBench addresses a more diagnostic question: when resource budgets and evaluation criteria are controlled, which components drive attack success? Specifically, MT-JailBench decomposes multi-turn jailbreaks into evaluation function, attack strategy, prompt generation, refinement, and flow control modules, and implements existing attacks through standardized interfaces. Our experiments show that end-to-end attack success rates can conflate conditions with mechanisms responsible for performance. Resource budgets and evaluation functions substantially affect reported ASR, so direct comparisons require these factors to be fixed. Under controlled conditions, prompt generation explains most performance variation, while refinement and flow control can provide moderate gains. Recombining the strongest components yields CrescendoX , which improves over its source attacks across target models. This result validates the central premise of MT-JailBench: component-level analysis is not only explanatory but predictive. More broadly, progress in multi-turn red teaming should be measured through controlled comparisons that reveal which assumptions, budgets, judges, and mechanisms produce attack success, rather than aggregate ASR alone. 

Scope of evaluation and limitations. MT-JailBench should be interpreted as a framework for controlled comparison, not as an absolute measure of model safety. Our evaluation assumes a black-box, text-only threat model, harmful behaviors from HarmBench [ 24 ], LLM-based judges, and finite resource budgets. Measured attack success may change under different behavior sets, judges, resource budgets, access policies, API safeguards, or system-level guardrails.

14 A Broader Impact Statement 

This work aims to improve the safety and reliability of large language models by studying vulnerabilities that arise in multi-turn interactions. As LLMs are increasingly used as conversational agents, safety failures may emerge not from a single explicit prompt, but from objectives accumulated across turns. MT-JailBench provides a controlled framework for evaluating these failures and identifying which experimental choices and attack components drive reported success. As with prior work in security and red teaming, our results are dual-use. The same analyses that identify weaknesses in current systems could be misused to construct stronger attacks. However, systematic evaluation is necessary for building defenses that remain effective when attackers adapt over multiple turns. We therefore frame MT-JailBench as a tool for controlled comparison, component-level diagnosis, and future defense testing, rather than as an absolute measure of model safety. Overall, we expect this work to have a positive impact by improving the rigor of multi-turn red-teaming evaluations and supporting the development of defenses that account for accumulated con-versational context. 

# B Related Work 

Multi-turn Jailbreaks Prior work on LLM jailbreaks can be broadly divided into single-turn and multi-turn attacks. While early studies focus on single-turn prompt design [ 53 , 23 , 30 , 6], recent research shows that distributing harmful intent across multiple turns is more effective and harder to detect. Among multi-turn jailbreaks, Crescendo [ 29 ] introduces a progressive interaction paradigm that gradually steers benign conversations toward harmful outputs through iterative context accumulation. Building on this idea, Chain of Attack (CoA) [ 39 ] models attacks as a semantic progression process, dynamically refining prompts based on target alignment and intermediate feedback. Foot-In-The-Door (FITD) [ 37 ] further interprets multi-turn jailbreaks through a psychological lens, leveraging incremental compliance to reduce refusal over successive turns. Reasoning-Augmented Conversation (RACE) [ 41 ] reframes harmful queries as benign reasoning tasks and models the attack as a structured reasoning process. Recent work further improves diversity and realism: ActorBreaker [ 27 ] replaces template-based prompts with semantically related “actor networks” to exploit distributional vulnerabilities, and X-Teaming [ 26 ] adopts a multi-agent framework for planning, execution, and optimization in multi-turn settings. Concurrently, emerging approaches emphasize automated planning, dynamic interaction, and broader exploration of attack trajectories [ 9, 3], enabling structured exploration and adaptive reasoning to expose vulnerabilities. 

Benchmarking Jailbreaks Prior benchmarks primarily focus on single-turn jailbreaks, with limited support for modeling multi-turn interaction dynamics. Among them, EasyJailbreak [ 51 ] provides a modular pipeline for jailbreaks using four components: Selector, Mutator, Constraint, and Evaluator. JailbreakBench [ 5] and HarmBench [ 24 ] standardize jailbreak evaluation, with HarmBench focusing on large-scale systematic comparison, and JailbreakBench emphasizing reproducibility through open artifacts and benchmarks. JailTrickBench [ 38 ] further studies the key factors influencing jailbreak performance. More recent work begins to consider multi-turn settings. OpenRT [ 34 ] extends evaluation to multi-turn jail-breaks by examining whether models can resist progressively escalated attacks and context accumulation. In contrast, our work introduces a unified benchmark that decomposes multi-turn jailbreaks into standard-ized components, enabling controlled, component-wise analysis and scalable development of new methods. 

Jailbreak Defenses Guardrail-based defenses aim to detect and block unsafe behavior. Early guard models, such as Llama Guard [ 15 ], Qwen3Guard [ 46 ], and WildGuard [ 13 ], operate in a single-turn manner by classifying queries and responses independently, without leveraging conversational context. Recent approaches extend this paradigm to multi-turn settings by incorporating dialogue history. For instance, Neural Barrier Function (NBF) [ 14 ] models dialogue as a dynamical system to prevent unsafe transitions, while Temporal Context Awareness (TCA) [ 19 ] tracks cumulative risk across turns. Overall, 15 guardrail methods rely on accurate detection and typically intervene once harmful intent becomes evident. Processing-based defenses mitigate jailbreaks by transforming inputs or outputs to weaken adversarial control. Input-side methods include paraphrasing [ 16 ] and randomized smoothing (e.g., SmoothLLM [ 28 ], SemanticSmooth [ 17 ]), while output-side methods apply post-hoc correction [ 35 ] or filtering (e.g., Aligner [ 18 ], ProAct [ 48 ]). Their effectiveness relies on the assumption that the model can correctly reject or neutralize harmful content after transformation. Internal defenses enhance jailbreak robustness by modifying the model itself, either through safety-focused training and alignment (e.g., Goal Prioritization [ 44 ], Safety-Tuned LLaMAs [ 4], Safe RLHF [ 8], Safe Unlearning [ 45 ]) or by intervening in in-ternal representations during inference (e.g., Circuit Breakers [ 52 ], LED [ 47 ], DETAM [ 20 ], Shaping Safety Boundaries [ 11 ]), but typically require white-box access, limiting their applicability to closed-source APIs. 

# C MT-JailBench System Design 

This section describes the engineering design of MT-JailBench and explains how modularity is achieved through standardized programming interfaces. 

## C.1 Attack Interfaces 

MT-JailBench operationalizes the five-module attack decomposition through three core interfaces: 

PromptGenerator , OutcomeEvaluator , and FlowController . Each attack implemented in the frame-work must instantiate all three interfaces, while the framework handles execution details such as target-model interaction, conversation-state tracking, and coordination across stages. 1. The PromptGenerator interface unifies attack-prompt generation and refinement. It produces the next attack prompt conditioned on the harmful objective, the current conversation history, and any evaluation signals from prior turns. It also supports prompt refinement under non-linear control flows, such as retrying failed turns or revisiting earlier turns in the conversation. 2. The OutcomeEvaluator interface determines whether the attack has satisfied its success criterion and may additionally return richer evaluation signals. In its simplest form, it outputs a binary success indicator. More advanced implementations may incorporate harmfulness scores, semantic similarity metrics, or auxiliary feedback derived from the target model’s responses. 3. The FlowController interface governs the attack trajectory by selecting the next action given the current state and evaluation results. MT-JailBench supports a flexible set of control-flow actions, including advancing to the next turn, retrying the current turn, reverting to a previous turn, and terminating with success or failure. It also supports more sophisticated strategies, such as performing multiple retries and selecting the highest-scoring outcome before proceeding. 

## C.2 Defense Interfaces 

Mainstream jailbreak defenses can be broadly categorized into two complementary paradigms: prompt pre-processing and response post-processing, where “pre” and “post” denote the defense’s position relative to the target-model interaction. Prompt pre-processing operates on the input side: it analyzes and, when appropriate, transforms user prompts before they are passed to the target model. Such defenses may block malicious prompts or expose malicious intent within the prompt, emphasizing prevention by neutralizing jailbreak attempts before they reach the model. Response post-processing, in contrast, operates on the output side. It evaluates responses generated by the target model and intervenes when unsafe or policy-violating content is detected. Such interventions may include rewriting the response, filtering specific content, or replacing the response entirely with a refusal message, such as “Sorry, I cannot assist with your request.” These defenses emphasize mitigation by addressing harmful outputs after they have been produced. ## C.3 Workflow Orchestration 

MT-JailBench structures the jailbreak workflow into discrete turns , each corresponding to one round of interaction among the attack components, optional defense components, and the target model. In each turn, the framework executes the following six steps, as illustrated in Figure 6. 1. Prompt generation : PromptGenerator produces the attack prompt. 2. Prompt pre-processing : TextProcessor may sanitize or transform the prompt before it reaches the target model. 3. Response generation : The target LLM generates a response to the processed prompt. 4. Response post-processing : TextProcessor may filter, rewrite, or replace the response if unsafe content is detected. 5. Outcome evaluation : OutcomeEvaluator determines whether the attack succeeded and may return auxiliary signals, such as harmfulness scores, to guide subsequent steps. 6. Flow control : FlowController selects the next action based on the evaluation results. Depending on this decision, the framework may advance to the next turn, retry the current turn, roll back to a previous turn, or terminate the workflow.

## D.1 Detailed Analysis on Judge Performance 

In Section 4.2, we show that the choice of evaluation function can substantially affect the reported ASR. To further analyze the performance and decision boundaries of CoA-Binary , FITD-Binary , and 

Score-Judge , we measure the ASR of each attack when using only a single judge for cross-validation. Interestingly, although CoA-Binary and FITD-Binary are both more permissive and generally report similar ASRs, they disagree substantially on the FITD attack: CoA-Binary reports less than half the ASR reported by FITD-Binary . This discrepancy suggests that attack-specific judges may overfit to the attacks they are designed to evaluate, thereby inflating reported ASRs.

# E Detailed Comparison of Multi-turn Jailbreak Components 

In this section, we present a structured comparison of representative multi-turn jailbreak methods through the lens of the modular formulation introduced in Sec. 3.2. We decompose each approach into five core components, including attack strategy, prompt generation, refinement, evaluation, and flow control. This perspective exposes key design differences that are often obscured in end-to-end implementations. 

Crescendo: 

• Attack Strategy: A fixed prompt template with few-shot examples for attack prompt generation. 

• Prompt Generation: The first-turn prompt is generated using a fixed prompt template, and subsequent prompts are generated based on the conversation history. 

• Prompt Refinement: The prompt is updated by the attacker LLM based on the target model’s response. 

• Evaluation Function: In our implementation, Crescendo uses the same 5-score judge as in XTeaming and ActorBreaker. 

• Control Policy: In our implementation, Cresceendo uses a refusal-guided policy that retries when the judge assigns a score of 1, and otherwise proceeds to the next turn. 

Chain-of-Attack: 

• Attack Strategy: A fixed prompt template with few-shot examples for attack prompt generation. 

• Prompt Generation: CoA pre-generates multiple candidate chains (i.e., a set of attack sequences) and selects one based on the range of semantic similarity scores to the harmful target, preferring wider ranges. The selected chain is then used as the attack queries and executed sequentially. 

• Prompt Refinement: The prompt is updated by the attacker LLM based on the target model’s response, while maintaining a similarity score close to the original. 

• Evaluation Function: CoA uses a binary judge (0/1) to determine success. 

• Control Policy: A similarity-guided policy that computes three semantic similarity scores after each turn: 

– sem : similarity between the current response and the harmful target 

– posted_sem : similarity from the previous turn 

– base_sem : similarity computed using a response generated without prior conversation history, measuring the effect of accumulated context 20 The policy proceeds when sem exceeds both base_sem and posted_sem , backtracks when it is lower than both, and otherwise retries the current turn. 

Foot-in-the-Door: 

• Attack Strategy: Fixed prompt templates that start from benign queries via transformation and progressively generate intermediate queries with increasing semantic proximity to the harmful behavior. 

• Prompt Generation: FITD pre-generates multiple trajectories (i.e., sets of attack sequences) and merges them into a single trajectory, which is then executed sequentially. 

• Prompt Refinement: FITD applies two mechanisms: Re-align , which makes the target response more closely aligned to the prompt, and Slippery Slope Paraphrase , which inserts an intermediate step to smooth transitions between successive turns. 

• Evaluation Function: FITD uses a binary judge (“safe” vs. “unsafe”) to determine success. 

• Control Policy: A refusal-guided policy that retries when refusal is detected via string matching (e.g., “I can’t”, “I’m sorry”). Upon refusal, it invokes an LLM to determine alignment: if misaligned, apply Re-align ; otherwise, apply Slippery Slope Paraphrase .

ActorBreaker: 

• Attack Strategy: A fixed prompt template first extracts multiple “actors” from the harmful behavior, which are then used as anchors in a second template to dynamically condition attack generation. 

• Prompt Generation: Conditioned on a selected “actor”, it generates an attack sequence, which are executed sequentially. 

• Prompt Refinement: The prompt is updated by the attacker LLM based on the target model’s response and the initial attack queries. 

• Evaluation Function: ActorBreaker uses a 5-score judge to determine success. 

• Control Policy: A refusal-guided policy that retries when a binary judge indicates rejection, and otherwise proceeds to the next turn. If the attack still fails after the final turn, it restarts from turn 1 with a different actor. 

XTeaming: 

• Attack Strategy: Instead of the “actor” used in ActorBreaker, XTeaming employs an LLM to generate more structured conditioning signals, including persona, context, approach, and turn-level plan, which are collectively referred to as strategies. These strategies serve as anchors to dynamically condition attack generation. 

• Prompt Generation: The first-turn prompt is generated using a turn-level plan, followed by dynamic generation of subsequent prompts conditioned on the conversation history and the plan. 

• Prompt Refinement: XTeaming employs TextGrad, which treats natural language feedback as gradients. It consists of three LLM-backed steps: (1) forward pass to produce a textual loss, (2) backward pass to generate textual gradients (feedback), and (3) an update step that refines the prompt using this feedback. 

• Evaluation Function: XTeaming uses a 5-score judge to determine success. 

• Control Policy: A score-guided policy that advances only when the current score exceeds the best score observed so far. If the attack still fails after the final turn, it restarts from turn 1 with a different strategy. 21 F Judge Prompts

23 G Detailed Experiment Setup 

In this section, we provide detailed descriptions of the experimental settings used in our benchmark. For all experiments, we use Qwen-2.5-32B [ 33 ] as the attacker LLM for prompt generation and refinement, and GPT-4o as the judge LLM for evaluation. 

## G.1 Setup for Section 4.1-4.3 

Under each setting (limited and abundant), we impose constraints on both the maximum number of turns ( max_turns ) and target-model interactions ( max_interactions ). We also constrain each attack’s internal resource usage (e.g., the number of attack strategies generated) and flow control behavior (e.g., the maximum number of retries per turn), as detailed below. 

Limited (L) Setting. (max_turns : 5, max_interactions : 10) 1. Crescendo: Allows up to 3 retries per turn, excluding the initial attempt. 2. CoA: Generates a single initial chain (i.e., one attack sequence) and uses it throughout the attack. During each retry, it may generate up to three refined prompts before proceeding with the best one, selected by semantic similarity. 3. FITD: Generates a single initial trajectory (i.e., one attack sequence). When generating the initial benign prompt, it allows up to 2 attempts if the initial prompt elicits a refusal. 4. Actor: Generates a single attack sequence using one actor. If the plan fails, the attack terminates. 5. XTeaming: Uses a single strategy (i.e., one attack sequence). If the strategy fails, the attack terminates. It also allows up to 3 retries per turn, excluding the initial attempt. 

Abundant (A) Setting. (max_turns : 5, max_interactions : 50) 1. Crescendo: Allows up to 10 retries per turn, excluding the initial attempt. 2. CoA: Generates 3 initial chains (i.e., 3 sets of attack sequences) and selects the best one. During each retry, it may generate up to 5 refined prompts before being required to proceed with the best one, selected by semantic similarity. 3. FITD: Generates 3 initial trajectories (i.e., 3 sets of attack sequences). When generating the initial benign prompt, it allows up to 10 attempts if the initial prompt elicits a refusal. 4. Actor: Generates 5 sets of attack sequences using 5 actors. If one plan fails, the method switches to the next actor and restarts the attack. 5. XTeaming: Uses 10 strategies (i.e., 10 sets of attack sequences). If one strategy fails, the method switches to the next strategy and restarts the attack. It also allows up to 3 retries per turn, excluding the initial attempt. 

## G.2 Setup for Section 4.4 

Section 4.4 sets max_turns to 5 and max_interactions to 20 to allow for sufficient exploration, and allows up to 3 retries per turn. We use GPT-4o as the target LLM. To conduct the experiment, we decomposed each attack into three fully independent components: prompt generation, prompt refinement, and flow control. This decomposition allows flexible recombination of the three components to construct a complete attack. However, components from different attacks are not naturally interoperable. To enable cross-attack composition under a unified framework, we introduced a set of simplifications. Importantly, these modifications are designed to preserve the core characteristics of each method as much as possible, while ensuring compatibility across components. The resulting simplifications are described below. 24 Simplifications for prompt generation. 

1. Crescendo prompt generation: Same as in Appendix E. 2. CoA prompt generation: Generates only one initial attack chain (i.e., one set of attack prompts). 3. FITD prompt generation: Generates only one initial trajectory (i.e., one set of attack prompts). 4. Actor prompt generation: Generates attack queries based on only one actor. 5. XTeaming prompt generation: Generates attack queries based on the first one attack strategy. 

Simplifications for prompt refinement. 

1. Crescendo prompt refinement: Same as in Appendix E. 2. CoA prompt refinement: The original CoA uses the pre-generated attack sequence during refinement. Since not all attacks pre-generate such a sequence (e.g., Crescendo), we instead use the prompt from the conversation history. The original CoA updater attempts to generate a new prompt whose semantic similarity to the harmful target exceeds a threshold. We retain this behavior and cap the number of attempts at 5, including the first attempt. This preserves fairness, as the retry process does not involve invoking the target model directly. 3. FITD prompt refinement: The original FITD uses two major refinement strategies: Re-align 

and Slippery Slope Paraphrase . We implement only Slippery Slope Paraphrase , where an intermediate turn is inserted to make the transition smoother upon refusal, as it better captures the core methodology of FITD and is more compatible with other attacks. The original FITD also checks the target model for refusal and regenerates accordingly. To ensure fairness, we removed this early refusal check, since it bypasses the framework and interacts with the target model directly. 4. Actor prompt refinement: We discard the output_type parameter in its refinement template for compatibility, as it is specific to ActorBreaker. This parameter controls the format of the generated refinement output, but is not required for other attacks. 5. XTeaming prompt refinement: No changes. TextGrad is naturally compatible with other attacks. 

Simplifications for flow control. 

1. Crescendo flow control: Same as in Appendix E. 2. CoA flow control: The original CoA flow control keeps retrying or backtracking until it either succeeds or exhausts available resources. For fairness, we removed this infinite loop. In addition, we replaced backtracking with retrying to maintain compatibility with other attacks. The final simplified logic is: continue if the current semantic similarity score is higher than both the previous turn’s score and the base_score (obtained by invoking the target model without conversation history); otherwise, retry. 3. FITD flow control: Same as in Appendix E. 4. Actor flow control: Same as in Appendix E. 5. XTeaming flow control: Same as in Appendix E.
