## Abstract 

Jailbreak prompts can trigger harmful comple-tions on aligned LLMs, In accordance, safety steering has been proposed: test-time activation interventions that steer jailbreak activations to trig-ger refusal while preserving benign utility. How-ever, existing steering methods are fundamentally supervised and tied to a static, limited training set, whereas real jailbreaks evolve and are often out-of-distributed from the training set, leading to failures on unseen attacks. In this paper, we tackle the failure on unseen jail-breaks problem, base on unsupervised latent di-rection discovery. We propose a bi-level adver-sarial training framework for zero-shot jailbreak defense. In the inner step, we simulate diverse jail-broken activations by extrapolating from refusal-state harmful-request activations via unsupervised latent direction discovery, which expands the cov-erage of real jailbreak activation subspaces. In the outer step, we train a potential-induced steering field to push these adversarial jailbroken states into refusal regions while keeping benign un-changed. Across three LLMs and six classical jailbreak families, our method achieves strong de-fense with attack success rates mostly below 5%, and rising subspace coverage throughout training helps explain the improved generalization.

To defend against jail-breaks, the LLM safety research community had recently identified safety steering as a promising direction, which enhances robustness at the representation level: instead of retraining the entire model, one learns steering directions or transformation modules in hidden spaces for behavior control. At test time, safety steering pushes activations that can trigger harmful responses towards refusal regions while leaving benign activations intact to preserve utility (Arditi et al., 2024; Wollschl ¬®ager et al., 2025; Shen et al., 2024b; Lee et al., 2024; Sheng et al., 2025). However, a key limitation of prior safety steering is its heavy reliance on a static, incomplete training set. This reliance either yields a global intervention that harms utility on be-nign tasks (Lee et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2024a; Wollschl ¬®ager et al., 2025), or a learned conditional intervention that fits limited classes of known jailbreaks and fail to generalize to unseen, and potentially out-of-distribution jailbreaks (Sheng et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). As a result, while condi-tional steering performs well within the training distribution, the defense effectiveness degrades significantly on unseen jailbreaks, as we further discuss in Section 3.1. Hence, the key limitation induces the following research question: 

Research Question. How to train safety steering, such that the defense can maximally generalize beyond the static, and limited training support ? 

Motivations. Inspired by unsupervised latent direction discovery (Mack & Turner, 2024a;b; Y ¬®uksel et al., 2021), we address this question via unsupervised jailbroken activa-tions simulation. Specifically, unsupervised latent direction discovery elicit large and consistent behavioral shifts across prompts. These transformations allow us to extrapolate from original harmful activations from refusal states. The extrapolation leads to a broader family of latent states that plausibly correspond to unseen jailbroken states that com-ply and exactly respond to harmful requests, as we discuss further in Section 3.2. Challenge 1: Safety steering need be expressive enough to adapt to unseen jailbreaks.

## 5. Bi-level Adversarial Training 

5.1. Overview 

We learn a scalar potential fœï : Rd ‚Üí R whose gradient induces a test-time steering field, and train it with a bi-level adversarial objective aligned with the three desiderata stated in the introduction: (i) general steer-ability , (ii) benign zero steer-ability , and (iii) jailbroken strong steer-ability . Our key design is to make property (iii) adversarially stress-tested : an inner step simultaneously (a) learns multiple latent directions via unsupervised latent direction discovery, and (b) uses these transforms to construct hard jailbroken activations that are the hardest to steer under the current potential; the outer step then updates fœï to satisfy all three properties jointly.

This supports the intended zero steer-ability : the steering field is designed to be near-zero on benign activations and strong only in (simulated/real) jailbreak neighborhoods. 

## 8. Mechanistic Interpretation (RQ2) 

Simulated jailbroken states progressively expand and cover real jailbreak regions. Figure 4 visualizes the representation-space evolution of our simulated jailbroken activations across training steps. Early in training, simu-lated points remain close to the original supervised support (harmful/refusal-adjacent states), reflecting that the current inner-step directions can only produce limited extrapolation. As bi-level training proceeds, the inner step adversarially searches latent directions that yield hard-to-steer simulated states, i.e., regions where the current potential field exhibits weak gradients. This pressure drives simulated activations to diffuse outward into previously under-defined areas of the activation manifold, which increasingly overlaps the empirical support of real jailbreak activations. 

Growing steer-ability on real jailbreak activations. 

While Figure 4 explains how the training support is ex-panded, Figure 5 shows how this translates into strong steer-ability on real jailbreak activations. At early steps, many jailbreak trajectories fail to enter the refusal region within the K-step steering budget, consistent with initially under-extrapolated fields. As train-ing advances, an increasing fraction of jailbreak activations are steered and fall close to the refusal cluster, indicating that the learned field had learnt strong steer-ability on pre-viously unknown jailbreak activations. Overall, Figure 4 explains how the inner step generates ad-versarial activation that extrapolates training support, then Figure 5 explains how the outer step learns the correspond-ing strong steer-ability on those simulated jailbroken activa-tions, thereby transfering to real jailbreak defenses.

## 10. Conclusion 

We addressed a key limitation of supervised safety steering: real jailbreaks often lie outside the activation support cov-8Steering Beyond the Support: Adversarial Training on Unsupervised Jailbroken Activation Simulation 

ered by training data, leading to weak steering gradients and poor robustness. We proposed a bi-level adversarial training framework that (i) synthesizes diverse jailbroken states in representation space via unsupervised latent directions and (ii) learns a spatially-varying steering field as the gradient of a potential to steer these states toward refusal while keeping benign activations unchanged. Experiments across models and jailbreak families show improved jailbreak robustness with minimal degradation of benign utility, and mechanistic analyses support that progressively expanding simulated-state coverage is a decisive driver of performance. 

## Impact Statement 

This work aims to improve the robustness of aligned large language models against unknown or out-of-distribution jail-break attacks by learning a test-time steering mechanism in representation space that redirects potentially harmful inter-nal states toward refusal behaviors while preserving benign utility. If deployed responsibly, it could reduce real-world risks of harmful content generation and provide a more scal-able framework for evaluating and training defenses beyond a fixed set of known attacks. At the same time, the same techniques could be misused to probe model vulnerabilities or synthesize stronger jailbreak signals, so we recommend safeguards such as controlled access, thorough red-teaming, careful release of code/checkpoints, and monitoring to en-sure the net effect is improved safety rather than accelerated misuse.

## A. Limitations and Future Work 

Limitations. Our framework critically relies on the quality of unsupervised latent direction discovery to simulate jailbreak activations beyond the supervised support. While we empirically demonstrate that the discovered directions meaningfully expand coverage over real jailbreak subspaces, there is no formal guarantee that these simulated states exhaust all failure modes. In particular, jailbreak behaviors that arise from non-natural languages, (e.g., base64, ciphers) may correspond to activation regions that are weakly reachable through linear latent interventions. This suggests an inherent limitation shared by activation-space extrapolation methods: robustness is bounded by the expressive capacity of the simulation mechanism itself. 

Future Work. It is valuable to explore richer jailbreak simulation mechanisms‚Äìsuch as non-linear latent transforms, like gradient based step-wise search, or linear composition of multiple latent directions‚Äìmay further expand coverage beyond what linear extrapolation achieves, and discover more complex jailbroken behaviors. In addition, while this work focuses on jailbreak robustness, the underlying idea of adversarially expanding representation-space support may generalize to other safety and robustness settings, such as hallucination control, unlearning tasks. 

## B. Related Work 

B.1. Safety Steering 

A growing line of work uses activation / representation steering to control safety behavior at test time. In (Arditi et al., 2024), aligned LLMs possess a one-dimensional ‚Äúrefusal direction‚Äù whose removal suppresses refusals and whose amplification elicits refusals even on benign prompts, effectively demonstrating a global linear subspace that mediates safety behavior. Building on this insight, (Lee et al., 2025; Shen et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2024) enhance model safety by amplifying the activation projection onto the identified refusal direction. However, indiscriminately adding this refusal direction on arbitrary input can only make the model globally more defensive, which inevitably harms benign task utilities. To overcome this, (Sheng et al., 2025) formulates refusal steering as a constrained optimization problem, learning a refusal vector that is effective on harmful and jailbreak inputs while imposing a principled null-space constraint so that benign representations are approximately unchanged, yet the underlying steering is still a single linear direction applied globally across activations. Overall, these methods unreliably assume that linear intervention trained from limited benign/harmful data pair suffices to generalize across unseen jailbreak strategies and OOD benign inputs. In contrast, our method adapts a small MLP potential function defined over hidden activations. This yields a more expressive nonlinear steering field that can better adapt to heterogeneous jailbreaks, while the representation redundancy allows us space to apply semantic equivalence constraint to extrapolate steering behaviors from training set activations to unseen OOD activations. 

B.2. Mechanistic direction discovery 

Another line of work with various developed steering methods (how to steer) is to discover meaningful directions (what to steer). (Y ¬®uksel et al., 2021) learns semantic directions in the latent space of pre-trained GANs using a contrastive objective over augmentations of unlabeled images, showing that meaningful editing axes can be discovered without supervision and reused across samples; (Dalva & Yanardag, 2024) extends this idea to text-to-image diffusion models, learning disentangled steering directions in the latent space. In language models, SAEs (Huben et al., 2024) trained on activations of LLMs and show that the learned dictionary features resolves superposition and enables causal interventions on specific behaviors. However, SAEs do not define a parametric mapping that takes small latent control vectors and produces structured transformations of downstream activations. To resolve this, (Mack & Turner, 2024a;b) explicitly model how early-layer nudges propagate to later-layer behavioral changes. In particular, (Mack & Turner, 2024b) learn unsupervised steering vectors and shallow MLP ‚Äútranscoders‚Äù that approximate the effect of intervening on a deep transformer slice, then use these transcoders to elicit latent behaviors such as hidden capabilities, tone changes, even jailbreak strategies. we inherit the idea that models admit semantic-equivalence directions along which high-level intent is preserved; we operationalize this by using discovered directions to extrapolate refusal activations to jailbroken ones. 12 Steering Beyond the Support: Adversarial Training on Unsupervised Jailbroken Activation Simulation

Following (Mack & Turner, 2024b), for each discovered latent direction v ‚àà Rd with ‚à•v‚à•2 = 1 , we generate simulated jailbroken activations by injecting the direction with a scalar magnitude R:

## E. Inference Time Computational Cost 

At inference time, our method performs test-time steering by iteratively updating a hidden activation h ‚àà Rd using the gradient of a learned potential function fœï : Rd ‚Üí R. Specifically, we apply K gradient-based update steps, where each step requires computing the input gradient ‚àáhfœï(h). Let P denote the number of parameters of the potential network fœï,implemented as a lightweight multi-layer perceptron (MLP). The computational cost of a single steering step consists of one forward pass and one backward pass with respect to the input h, which together incur a cost of O(P ). Therefore, the overall time complexity of test-time steering for a single sample is

## H. Impact of Gradient Update Steps 

We study how the number of gradient update steps K in test-time steering affects the safety‚Äìutility trade-off. Intuitively, larger 

K allows the gradient-induced steering field to perform stronger iterative correction on potentially jailbroken activations, but may also over-correct borderline benign inputs. Moreover, the marginal robustness gain diminishes at larger K (e.g., beyond roughly 16 ‚Äì32 steps), suggesting a practical budget that balances effectiveness and benign preservation.
