Abstract 

Despite rigorous safety alignment, Large Language Models (LLMs) remain vul-nerable to jailbreak attacks. Existing black-box methods often rely on heuris-tic templates or exhaustive trials, lacking mechanistic interpretability and query efficiency. In this study, we investigate an intrinsic vulnerability in the safety mechanisms of LLMs, where safety alignment relies on a small set of sparsely distributed attention heads, leaving much of the representational space weakly monitored. We formalize this phenomenon with a mathematical jailbreaking model that characterizes the delicate boundary of effective text obfuscation and analyt-ically explains observed jailbreak behaviors. Guided by this model, we propose Babel, an efficient black-box attack framework that exploits the identified safety gap through systematic obfuscation sampling with iterative, feedback-driven dis-tribution refinement, enabling reliable and high-success jailbreak attacks without access to model internals. Comprehensive evaluations on frontier commercial models demonstrate that Babel achieves state-of-the-art attack success rates and superior query efficiency. Specifically, compared to state-of-the-art methods, Babel increases the attack success rate on GPT-4o from 41.33% to 82.67% and on Claude-3-5-haiku from 38.33% to 78.33% within an average of 40 queries, providing a robust red-teaming methodology for LLMs safety research. 

1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as cornerstone computational technologies across diverse industrial and academic sectors [Hurst et al., 2024, OpenAI, 2024, Cai et al., 2024, Liu et al., 2024a]. The remarkable versatility and rapid evolution of LLMs have catalyzed their widespread commercial adoption, consistently extending both their practical scope and technical boundaries [Co-manici et al., 2025, Anthropic, 2024, xAI, 2025]. Despite their proliferation, the adversarial resilience of LLMs remains a critical concern, as their safety mechanisms often struggle to keep pace with evolving attack vectors. Certain adversarial attacks can go beyond the LLMs’ safety broundaries [Gao et al., 2025] which are not fully understood. In particular, LLMs have proven vulnerable to jailbreak attacks [Zhou et al., 2023, Chu et al., 2024a, Yu et al., 2023a], whereby meticulously crafted prompts bypass safety filters to elicit harmful or biased content. Even state-of-the-art models are not immune, occasionally producing prohibited outputs when subjected to sophisticated adversarial inputs [Yan et al., 2025, Yang et al., 2025a]. Consequently, rigorous red-teaming through diverse jailbreak methodologies has become imperative, serving as a vital diagnostic tool to evaluate and fortify the safety boundaries of LLMs. Jailbreak attacks are generally categorized as white-box and black-box attacks based on attacker capabilities. Black-box attacks pose a more pervasive threat, as most leading-edge models are deployed as closed-source services [OpenAI, 2025, DeepMind, 2025]. Existing black-box attack

techniques primarily design methods from the attackers’ perspective, constructing and optimizing templates and prompts through extensive search or heuristic algorithms to achieve effective and transferable attacks [Yu et al., 2023b, Li et al., 2024, Chu et al., 2024b, Hughes et al., 2024, Yan et al., 2025]. However, these approaches often lack interpretability for ignoring vulnerabilities within the LLMs’ inherent safety mechanisms, limiting their performance and insights into enhancing safety. To address these limitations, we shift the perspective to the internal safety mechanisms of LLMs, aiming to uncover intrinsic vulnerabilities that can be systematically exploited. Prior work shows that safety alignment is primarily implemented by a small number of sparsely distributed attention heads (safety heads) [Zhou et al., 2024b], meaning that only a limited subset of hidden states is monitored while a large representational space remains unchecked. This imbalance creates an exploitable vulnerability. For the original harmful query, copyright-related information is captured by safety heads, resulting in refusal. In contrast, a carefully constructed obfuscated input can shift harmful semantics into hidden states not covered by safety heads, thereby bypassing safety alignment and eliciting disallowed outputs. However, the boundary of effective obfuscation is highly delicate: excessive obfuscation destroys harmful intent, whereas insufficient obfuscation remains detectable. To characterize this boundary, we propose a mathematical jailbreaking model that quantifies obfuscation strength, enabling reliable evaluation and controlled optimization of obfuscation strategies that stably activate this vulnerability. Guided by this insight, we propose Babel, a black-box attack framework that bypasses safety attention via text obfuscation. Babel evaluates and optimizes the obfuscation distribution of harmful queries, explicitly targeting the identified safety gap. Unlike existing trial-and-error prompting approaches, Babel performs systematic obfuscation sampling, regulating the statistical properties of obfuscation to maximize bypass probability. This process is further guided by an iterative feedback loop that dynamically refines the sampling distribution based on model responses. Extensive experiments on state-of-the-art closed-source models show that Babel achieves state-of-the-art attack success rates: 82.67% on GPT-4o, 78.33% on Gemini-2.0-Flash, 95.67% on Grok-3, and 75.67% on Claude-3.5-Haiku, while substantially improving query efficiency over existing baselines. This paper makes the following contributions: • We identify a intrinsic vulnerability in the safety mechanisms of LLMs and formalize it through a mathematical model that characterizes how to bypass safety alignment. • We propose Babel, a black-box jailbreak framework that systematically exploits this vulner-ability via distribution-aware obfuscation sampling and iterative optimization. • We conduct extensive evaluations on commercial LLMs from multiple providers, achieving state-of-the-art attack success rates with significantly improved query efficiency. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Safety Mechanisms of LLMs 

Recent mechanistic interpretability studies have begun to uncover the internal structures underlying safety alignment in large language models [Zhou et al., 2024b, Chen et al., 2024, Wei et al., 2024]. Prior work suggests that safety behaviors are implemented by sparse and highly localized components, including specific attention heads and neurons, whose ablation can substantially weaken safety alignment while largely preserving general model utility. For example, Zhou et al. [2024b] identify safety-critical attention heads whose removal significantly increases harmful responses despite modifying only a negligible fraction of parameters. Similarly, Chen et al. [2024] show that fewer than 1% of neurons consistently contribute to handling harmful queries, indicating that safety alignment is concentrated in compact parameter subspaces. Subsequent studies further reveal the structural fragility of these mechanisms. Wei et al. [2024] demonstrate that safety-critical regions occupy only a small fraction of the parameter space and can be disrupted through pruning or low-rank modifications with minimal impact on downstream utility. Related work also shows that neuron-level pruning can severely degrade alignment behavior even under small perturbations, highlighting the brittleness of safety-relevant representations. Moreover, recent evidence suggests that post-training capabilities, such as enhanced reasoning behaviors in LRMs, may partially suppress or mask pre-existing safety mechanisms rather than eliminate them entirely, implying a complex interaction between reasoning and alignment objectives. 2Together, these findings suggest that current LLM safety mechanisms rely on compact yet fragile internal structures that remain vulnerable to targeted perturbations. This line of work motivates further investigation into how reasoning dynamics interact with safety-critical representations and how such mechanisms fail under adversarial prompts. 

2.2 Jailbreak Attack 

White-box methods that generates adversarial suffixes based on gradient-based prompt optimiza-tion [Zou et al., 2023b, Yang et al., 2025b], offerring high interpretability but low transferability. Their requirement for internal model structure parameters and gradient information limits their per-formance. Black-box methods are more diverse, manually crafted jailbreak prompts circulate actively on various online forums and social media [reddit, 2025], posing a threat to the commercial LLMs. Heuristic-based methods search jailbreak prompts within a massive input space [Liu et al., 2023, Hughes et al., 2024]. Model-aware and attention mechanism based methods achieve transferability across different models [Pu et al., 2024, Lin et al., 2025]. There are also methods that construct attack prompts directly based on specific rules, capable of successfully executing a jailbreak in a single attempt [Liu et al., 2024b, Zheng et al., 2024]. While these methods are effective on both open-source and closed-source models, they overlook the internal safety mechanisms of the model, resulting in their inability to balance effectiveness and efficiency. 

3 LLM Safety Mechanism and Jailbreaking Model 

In this section, we first analyze the safety mechanisms of the open-source LLMs and discuss how to exploit the vulnerabilities of them under black-box scenarios to bypass it. Then we introduce a mathematical model to describe the process of jailbreaking by bypass safety mechanisms and validate the its validity through empirical experiments. 

3.1 Intrinsic Vulnerability in LLM’s Safety Alignment 

Researches have found that removing a single safety head will increase the changes of jailbreaking to aligned models like Llama-2-7b-chat [Zhou et al., 2024b, Touvron et al., 2023]. Additionally, LLMs can identify harmful inputs in early layers and alignment causes models to generate substantial negative sentiment predictions in intermediate attention layers, which are then refined into specific rejection tokens for safe generation [Zhou et al., 2024a]. Therefore, the immediate thought is to prevent the security attention heads in these early layers from detecting harmful behavior, thereby bypassing the security mechanisms to achieve jailbreaking. Intuitively, altering more characters in a malicious query makes its malicious behavior harder to detect, thereby increasing the likelihood of bypassing security headers. To verify that this intuition holds true in the black-box model, we conducted a series of empirical studies on the GPT-4o model. 3As shown in the Figure 1, the inputs consist of both harmless and malicious queries, and the model can respond to harmless queries. Altering only a few characters in the harmless query causes the security header to fail to understand the harmless query, thereby refusing to respond to the harmful query. However, when the harmless query remains unchanged, as the number of altered characters in the harmful query increases, the model regains the ability to understand the harmless query and gradually resumes responding to harmless requests. This phenomenon indicates that the security header does not identify the complete semantics ultimately understood by the model. Consequently, there is a gap between the model’s security mechanism response and its final output, creating an exploitable vulnerability. By appropriately modifying harmful queries, attackers can prevent the security header from recognizing the harmful behavior, while the model ultimately produces the harmful output.

4.1 Harmful Query Obfuscation 

To find the distribution with the highest probability density within the jailbreaking interval, we propose a systematic obfuscation method that generates different obfuscation degree distributions. Generally, string perturbations have a smaller impact on LLM comprehension than token perturbations. The obfuscation targets are grouped into three categories, string, token, and sequence, which enables control of the obfuscation degree at multiple granularities. The specific obfuscation operations and procedures are detailed in the appendix B. 

4.2 Harmful Query Embedding 

This design aims to further suppress safety heads and increase the likelihood of jailbreaking. Given the model’s availability requirements, harmless requests should not be rejected by the model. We attempt to embed obfuscated harmful queries within harmless requests to avoid rejection. Specifically, we define four categories of harmless requests: Translation , Statistics , Transformation and Retrieval ,requiring the model to perform different operations respectively on the obfuscated harmful query be-fore executing it. In addition, prompting model to start with "Sure" can improve the performance [Zou et al., 2023a, Wei et al., 2023], therefore we build a template to combine harmless requests with harmful queries and prompt how the model begins. The specific template of harmful query embedding can be find in the appendix A.

5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setting Target models and settings. To validate the ef-fectiveness of our method against closed-source models under a fully black-box setting, we se-lecte 4 commercially available LLMs that are both highly capable and widely used, including gpt-4o-2024-11-20, Gemini-2.0-flash, Claude-3-5-haiku-20241120, and grok-3 [Hurst et al., 2024, Comanici et al., 2025, xAI, 2025, Anthropic, 2024]. The target model’s temperature was uni-formly set to 1 and no system prompts were used. 

Attackers’ ability. Attackers possess only black-box access to models. They can input attack prompts and obtain returned outputs by calling the model’s commercial API. 

Datasets and evaluation metrics. We evaluate on the Harmbench [Mazeika et al., 2024], which comprises 300 harmful queries. We use Attack Success Rate (ASR) to evaluate the effectiveness and Aeverage Number of Requests (ANR) to evaluate the efficiency. Harmfulness assessment of model outputs is automatically performed by gpt-4o, whose corresponding prompt templates originate from Hughes et al. [2024]. Following evaluation, we manually verify all outputs flagged as harmful to eliminate false positives potentially introduced by LLM evaluation. Since each harmful query is not executed just once when evaluating the attack success rate across the entire dataset, the success rate is generally representative. Therefore, we do not conduct multiple experiments here, and the results do not include statistical measures such as variance. 

Baselines. We select four strong baseline methods for comparison [Hughes et al., 2024, Lin et al., 2025, Liu et al., 2024b, Mehrotra et al., 2024]. Although there are other methods such as GCG, AutoDAN, and PAIR [Zou et al., 2023b, Liu et al., 2023, Chao et al., 2025], they have been surpassed 7by more recent methods and are therefore not included in our evaluation. Since Bon requires a large number of queries to reach its best performance, incurring prohibitive costs. we restrict its query count to 50. We keep the original settings of baselines and modify only the target model and dataset. 5.2 Effectiveness of Attack 

The results in Table 1 show that although the four baseline methods achieve relatively high ASRs on certain models, their overall performance remains limited. Bon, which relies solely on random obfuscation, performs poorly across all models when the number of queries is constrained. PiF is effective on Gemini-2.0-flash and Grok-3, reaching ASRs of 72.67% and 82.00% respectively, by exploiting attention mechanisms to obscure malicious intent. However, its weaker results on other models indicate limited transferability. FlipAttack exhibits more stable performance across models, suggesting moderate versatility, but its attack effectiveness remains modest. By contrast, Babel achieves the highest ASRs on all four models, reaching 82.67%, 78.33%, and 75.67% on GPT-4o, Gemini-2.0-flash, and Claude-3.5-Haiku, respectively, and up to 95.67% on Grok-3. Figure 7 demonstrates the obfuscation distribution of the jailbreaking samples. By contrast to Table 1, it is suggested that Grok-3 is more susceptible to jailbreak attacks, as its jailbreak samples exhibit a lower distribution, indicating that its safety mechanisms are easier to bypass. The results of comparative methods fail to reveal this characteristic. Therefore, as a red team approach, Babel can not noly effectively jailbreak LLMs with transferability, but also evaluate the safety of the model. 

5.3 Efficiency of Attack 

Although methods like FlipAttack achieve jailbreaking with a single request, it comes at the cost of limited effectiveness and scalability. So we exclude such methods from the efficiency comparison. While achieving the highest ASRs, Babel still maintained a low number of requests. For Bon, the number of requests results from its random search process, meaning its effectiveness mainly depends on the search strategy and query volume, which leads to low efficiency. PiF requires multiple attempts to replace or insert tokens at different positions, thereby increasing its request count. In contrast, Babel handles requests more efficiently, each request both performs a jailbreak attempt and serves as a sampling point. As a result, Babel achieves the lowest ANRs on most models. 6 Conclusion 

This paper identifies the vulnerability in LLM’s safety mechanism caused by gap in safety heads coverage and proposes a mathematical model describing the jailbreak attack that exploits this vulnerability. Based on this model, we introduce Babel, a jailbreak framework that bypasses LLM safety mechanism through obfuscation and distribution-optimized sampling. Extensive experiments verify its effectiveness, determining that this black-box method exposes a critical vulnerability in LLMs and provides valuable insights for enhancing their safety. 9Limitations 

This work focuses on text-based jailbreak attacks, allowing us to provide a detailed analysis of the intrinsic safety mechanisms of large language models. While the insights gained may be relevant to other modalities or extension-based attacks, a systematic exploration in these directions is beyond the scope of this study. Similarly, we do not explicitly assess interactions with existing external defense strategies, as our primary aim is to understand model-level safety behaviors. We consider these extensions and evaluations as valuable directions for future research. 

Ethical Considerations 

This work is intended solely for defensive security research aimed at improving the safety of large language models. We did not deploy the method in real-world settings or facilitate malicious use. All experiments were conducted on publicly accessible commercial models for controlled red-teaming evaluation, no personal data was used, and code release is restricted to reduce misuse risk while supporting responsible disclosure and mitigation efforts. 

Broader Impacts 

Our study contributes positively by exposing concrete weaknesses in current safety alignment and by providing a methodology for evaluating whether commercial LLM safeguards fail under obfuscated harmful prompts. These findings can support stronger red-teaming, targeted mitigations, and more reliable safety evaluation pipelines. At the same time, jailbreak research is inherently dual use: attackers could adapt the high-level ideas to probe deployed models. To reduce that risk, we censor sensitive generations in the paper, avoid operational deployment guidance, and restrict code release while pursuing responsible disclosure.

A The Template of Harmful Query Embedding 

Harmful Query Embedding Template 

The request: <harmful query>. Please <harmless request>, respond to the request. Just output the <harmless request response> and the <harmful query response>. 

This is the prompt template we constructe for implementing harmful query embedding. We insert suffixes into the harmless behavior templates to explicitly guide the model’s output. This prevents the generation of semantically explicit harmful queries that could cause jailbreak failure. The specific details of the four harmless requests are as follows: • Translate. Instructing the model to translate the harmful query into another language and output the translated sentence. • Statistic. Instructing the model to count the number of specific characters within the harmful query text and output the statistical result. • Transformation. Tnstructing the model to perform a specific transformation on the harmful query text and output the transformed text. • Retrieval. Instructs the model to search for words in the harmful query that meets specific criteria and output that word. verbintherequest. The reason why we choose them is that they implicitly instruct the model to process obfuscated harmful queries as meaningful statements, preventing it from treating harmful queries as meaningless random characters.

B Algorithms 

B.1 Harmful Query Obfuscation Obfuscation objects. The objects of obfuscation are as follow. • String obfuscation. Includeing four specific methods: converting lowercase letters to uppercase, randomly replacing letters with the preceding or following character in the alphabet, swapping positions of two characters, and removing whitespace characters. 13 • Token obfuscation. Randomly replaces a single token while preserving sentence coherence. We use a masking model to predict the token to be replaced and randomly select a different token from the prediction probabilities for substitution. • Sequence obfuscation. This involves two operations: swapping the positions of two tokens and inserting tokens at random positions. Similar to token obfuscation, we insert [mask] at random positions within the original token sequence and use the masked model to predict the token to be inserted. 

Obfuscation level. We set upper limits on the number of tokens affected per obfuscation category, corresponding to their maximum obfuscation levels. The upper limit for capitalization conversion is set to half the number of letters in the harmful query, as a fifty-fifty mix of uppercase and lowercase letters is significantly harder to decipher than all-uppercase text. The quantity of targets for other obfuscation operations is determined by the current obfuscation level. 

Obfuscation sequence. To prevent character obfuscation from altering tokens and causing obfusca-tion between token and sequence obfuscation, we execute obfuscation operations in the sequence: token obfuscation → sequence obfuscation → character obfuscation. The obfuscation level adjusts the distribution of samples’ obfuscation degree by varying the quantity of different obfuscation objects. The specific sequence is shown in Algorithm 2. 

Algorithm 2 Textual Obfuscation                             

> Require: Harmful query B, obfuscation level l
> Ensure: Obfuscated text B′
> 1: s←OBFUSCATION LEVEL (l)
> 2: B′←INSERT TOP TOKENS (B, s )
> 3: B′←PERTURB TOP TOKENS (B′, s )
> 4: B′←EXCHANGE CHARACTERS (B′, s )
> 5: B′←BREAK SENTENCES (B′, s )
> 6: B′←ASCII PERTURBATION (B′, s )
> 7: B′←UPPERCASE PERTURBATION (B′, s )
> 8: return B′

B.2 Distribution-Optimized Sampling 

By examining samples from small-scale sampling, ones with high (low) obfuscation but model outputs of rejection (harmlessness) are removed. Then we estimate the upper and lower bounds of the model’s jailbreaking interval using the maximum obfuscation degree among rejected samples and the minimum obfuscation degree among harmless samples. During large-scale sampling, we attempt samples falling within the estimated jailbreaking interval, while continuously updating the trusted jailbreaking interval estimate. Throughout this process, we record the number of samples whose obfuscation degree do not align with their assigned interval. When this count reaches the threshold, the current jailbreaking interval estimate is deemed invalid. Then we skip current distribution and proceeds to the next level of obfuscation until jailbreaking or reaching the max obfuscation level. The overall process is shown in Algorithm 1.
