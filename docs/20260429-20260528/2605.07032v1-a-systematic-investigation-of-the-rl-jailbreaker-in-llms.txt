## Abstract 

The evolution of generative models from next-token predictors to autonomous engines of com-plex systems necessitates rigorous safety harden-ing. Adversarial jailbreaking, the strategic manip-ulation of models to elicit harmful output, remains a primary threat to safe deployment. While Rein-forcement Learning (RL) frames jailbreaking as a multi-step attack through sequential optimization, a mechanistic understanding of why the frame-work succeeds remains incomplete. To fill this gap, we present the first systematic decomposi-tion of RL jailbreaking. We deconstruct the frame-work into problem formalization (reward function, action space, episode length), and algorithmic measures (RL algorithm, training data, reward-shaping) to identify the structural determinants of adversarial success. Our results reveal that the RL-jailbreaker successfully compromised all targeted models and safeguards. Through this first-of-its-kind analysis, we demonstrate that environment formalization, specifically dense rewards and ex-tended episode lengths, is the primary driver of jailbreaking success. This work provides a tool for improving RL-jailbreaker efficiency and, ul-timately, harden generative models resistant to RL-based attacks. 

Warning: To demonstrate vulnerabilities, this paper contains unfiltered and potentially offensive jailbreaking examples. Reader discretion advised.

The rapid integration of generative models into digital ecosystems has paved the way for “Agentic AI”, where generative models are granted autonomous agency to inter-act with external tools, execute multi-step reasoning, and perform complex planning (Sapkota et al., 2026). As these

This move to real-world applications and global adoption introduces diverse training data, synthetic outputs, and per-sonalized user interactions, all of which add to the complex-ity of maintaining robust safety measures. Conducting a systematic assessment of jailbreaking methods provides the mechanistic insights necessary to accelerate the discovery of vulnerabilities and establishes a roadmap for hardening agentic systems against adversarial success. In many of those real-world applications, traditional static safety measures have often proven insufficient (Wei et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2024). Generative models remain suscepti-ble to jailbreaking , where input prompts are adversarially manipulated to elicit restricted or harmful outputs by by-passing safety measures and alignment constraints. As the interactions between generative models and the real-world become increasingly complex, jailbreaking is evolving from static prompt injections into an automated and dynamic process of discovery. Consequently, identifying vulnerabili-ties through systematic jailbreaking has become a critical prerequisite for ensuring the safe deployment of generative models. Traditional jailbreaking approaches have evolved from prompt engineering (Wei et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2024) and white-box gradient updates (Shin et al., 2020; Schwinn et al., 2024) toward sophisticated auto-mated frameworks (Mehrotra et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025b; Nasr et al., 2025). In this context, Reinforcement Learn-ing (RL) has emerged as an automated paradigm, where an adversarial agent treats the generative model as an envi-ronment, iteratively exploring the vast input space to maxi-mize a reward function tied to attack success. Despite the promise of RL-driven jailbreaking approaches, existing re-search (Deng et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024b;a) often adopts a generative model-centric view, treating the RL agent as a black-box tool rather than a complex system requiring rig-orous framing and algorithmic analysis. Our analysis reveals that the reward function and extending episode lengths are the primary factors affecting Attack Success Rate (ASR). 

## 2. Background and Related Works 

Safety Concerns with LLMs: As LLMs have grown in capability and adoption, so have concerns around their safety and potential for misuse (Weidinger et al., 2022). Two closely related lines of work have emerged to study these concerns: red-teaming , the practice of proactively attacking LLMs with the purpose of identifying their vul-nerabilities (Purpura et al., 2025), and jailbreaking , the act of making an LLM produce outputs that violate its safety or policy rules (Wei et al., 2023). Dedicated benchmark-ing datasets have been developed to evaluate model safety (R ¨ottger et al., 2025), and AI safety researchers have con-ducted structured red-teaming exercises to probe model vul-nerabilities (McKenzie et al., 2026). Defences against these threats have also been proposed at deployment time (Wang et al., 2025a), and the scope of jailbreaking has extended beyond text-only models: Vision Language Models (VLMs) and Audio Language Models (ALMs) have been shown to be susceptible to attack using simple best-of-n sampling strategies (Hughes et al., 2025). 

Automated Jailbreaking and Red-teaming of LLMs: 

Early jailbreaking relied on handcrafted prompt templates (Wei et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2024) and structured prompting strategies such as cipher-encoded inputs (Yuan et al., 2023) and tree-based iterative refinement (Mehrotra et al., 2024). While effective, this required considerable human effort and proved insufficient to break some language models, even over prolonged timescales (Sharma et al., 2025). One of the first cases of an RL algorithm automating this red-teaming leveraged an actor-critic algorithm to train an attacker LLM :a language model fine-tuned to generate adversarial prompts for a target LLM (Perez et al., 2022). Subsequent works predominantly used Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO; Schulman et al., 2017) to finetune attacker LLMs (Casper et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025b; Liu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). Group Relative Policy Optimization (Guo et al., 2025a) has also recently received attention for jail-breaking (Guo et al., 2025b; Nasr et al., 2025). Unlike these works, which use RL to finetune an LLM, RL-jailbreaker guided a search over the space of prompts using a PPO agent for decision making (Chen et al., 2024a), with a sparse re-ward based on cosine similarity to known harmful responses. SeqRL expanded on this, exploiting sequential interaction history to improve query efficiency (Yoon et al., 2025). This paper builds upon the automation framing of Chen et al. (2024a) to address two gaps we identified in prior work: the lack of a systematic, component-wise understanding of why RL jailbreaking succeeds, and the absence of principled evaluation against safeguard models. 

Safeguard Models: Safeguard models are trained to de-tect and block harmful prompts from a user and/or harmful responses from an LLM, with the harmful categories prede-fined at training time (Markov et al., 2023). While they have proven effective in practice, a range of widely deployed safe-guards have been shown to be vulnerable to jailbreaking in isolation, including: LlamaGuard, OpenAI’s API guardrails for GPT-3 and 4 (Zeng et al., 2024b), Claude v1, v2, and v3 (Li et al., 2024), the OpenChatKit moderation model, Nemo-Guardrails (Shen et al., 2024), and ShieldGemma (McKenzie et al., 2026). A notable exception is Anthropic’s constitutionally trained safeguard, which remained robust after over 3, 000 hours of attempted jailbreaking (Sharma et al., 2025). We examine the setting where a target model is augmented with such safeguard models to mimic deploy-ment conditions in our study of RL-based jailbreaking.

Cosine Similarity: To measure the effectiveness of a jail-break, we must quantify how closely a model’s generated output aligns with a “ground truth” target response. Sim-ple string matching is often insufficient, as two sentences can be semantically identical while sharing few identical words (Zhang et al., 2019). To overcome this, we project the text into a high-dimensional vector space where distances represent meaning (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) 1. Cosine similarity serves as the metric for this alignment, providing a proxy score of how well the agent has steered the generative model toward the intended adversarial output. ## 3. Methodology 

This section builds upon Chen et al. (2024a)’s empirical setup to study RL-based jailbreaking. We formalize the interaction between the RL agent and the LLM environment as a POMDP, and identify the axes for our experiments in Section 4. 

3.1. Environment Environment formalization: The observation is encoded as a d-dimensional, ℓ2-normalized embedding of the current harmful prompt template , concatenated with the current step index, a termination flag, and the previous action index. The action space is discrete, A = {GENERATE SIMILAR ,

CROSSOVER , EXPAND , SHORTEN , REPHRASE }. Each action instructs the helper LLM to rewrite the template, which is then instantiated with N harmful questions and sent to the target LLM. We study two reward functions: i) dense reward , as the average cosine similarity between the model output and the ground truth response embeddings rt ∈ [0 , 1] ,and ii) sparse reward function rt ∈ { 0, 1}, a binary signal providing rt = +1 for a cosine similarity > 0.7, and rt = 0 

otherwise, with full expressions in Appendix A.2.

Target models and safeguards: Our evaluation suite com-prises of four open-weight models: Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct, Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct (Grattafiori et al., 2024), Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 (Yang et al., 2025), and Tiny-aya-global (Sala-manca et al., 2026) 2. To simulate a defense environment, we wrap these models in safeguards (Llama-Guard (Chi et al., 2024), or ShieldGemma (Zeng et al., 2024a)) on both the prompt and response sides. 

3.2. Agent 

We train our policy using either PPO or Double Deep Q-Network (DDQN; van Hasselt et al., 2016), with the latter being untested in the RL-based red-teaming literature we ex-plored while developing this work. Policies and Q-functions are implemented as two-layer feedforward artificial neural networks, with hyperparameters detailed in Table 2. 

Episode lifecycle: Each episode starts by sampling N harm-ful questions, and selecting an initial template from a queue via Upper Confidence Bound-style Monte Carlo Tree Search (UCB-MCTS; Kocsis & Szepesv ´ari, 2006), where the queue is seeded with a fixed set of hand-crafted jailbreak tem-plates, and grows as successful mutations are appended during training, as done in Chen et al. (2024a) (see details in Appendix A.2). The agent then acts for up to T steps. At each step, the agent selects a mutation action, the helper model modifies the template, then passes the final prompt to the prompt safeguard or target model, and the environment returns the next observation from the target model or the re-sponse safeguard, along with a scalar reward that evaluates the attack attempt. 

Data and training: Adopting a methodology consistent with the RL-jailbreaker (Chen et al., 2024a), we utilize a subset of 20 harmful prompts from the AdvBench dataset (Zou et al., 2024), which contains responses from an un-aligned Vicuna model (Chiang et al., 2023) as the semantic “ground truth” target. The training phase is conducted across 

5 independent random seeds, and all results are reported with 95% bootstrapped confidence intervals (Efron, 1987).

## 4. Results and Discussion 

This section details our empirical findings, centered on two primary research objectives: quantifying the adversarial success of RL jailbreaking, and performing a mechanistic decomposition of the RL jailbreaking framework.

4.2. Structural decomposition 

In our second contribution 2, we perform a structural de-composition of the RL jailbreaking framework, starting with environment training, specifically, investigating the impact of the reward function . We evaluate two strategies: i) dense reward, as the average cosine similarity between the model output and the ground truth response embeddings 

rt ∈ [0 , 1] , providing a continuous feedback signal to guide the agent closer to the target; however, it could potentially trap the agent in local minima where semantic proximity is high, and ii) sparse reward function rt ∈ { 0, 1}, a binary signal providing rt = +1 for a cosine similarity > 0.7,and rt = 0 otherwise, could present a credit-assignment challenge due to the sparsity of the objective if the agent keep receiving zero rewards. Our decomposition reveals a distinct divergence in perfor-mance based on the target model: Llama (1B/3B) outper-forms under dense reward. Whereas using a sparse reward is more effective in Qwen and Tiny-aya models, as shown in Figure 2. We hypothesize that sparse rewards underperform in stronger target models (higher refusal rate) due to infre-quent feedback, which prevents PPO from establishing an effective learning signal. Although dense rewards address this through frequent shaping signals, they can hinder per-formance on simpler models if the dense proxy is poorly aligned with true success. 

Reward function and safeguard: We evaluate the im-pact of dense and sparse rewards on a multi-layered defen-sive pipeline, where a prompt safeguard is combined with a target model, followed by a response safeguard. This integrated architecture increases the difficulty for the jail-breaking agent, as these safeguards are explicitly trained to intercept and block harmful context at both the input and output stages. Episode length: Given the inherent strengths of RL in se-quential decision-making, we investigate how the episode length influences adversarial success. Within the RL-jailbreaker framework, an episode terminates after a fixed number of steps, at which point the context windows for the helper agent, target model, and safeguards are reset. This experiment aims to determine whether a longer episode provides the RL-jailbreaker with the necessary runway to execute complex, multi-turn attack sequences or if it pri-marily complicates the task through the credit assignment challenge. While the original RL-driven framework utilizes a fixed 5-step episode length, we vary this parameter across 

5, 10 , 25 , and 50 steps to observe its impact on performance. As illustrated in Figure 5, the results indicate that the op-timal episode length is model-dependent. In contrast, the 5-step baseline consistently underperforms, suggesting that Llama’s multi-layered safety alignments require more extensive, sustained interactions to success-fully bypass. In contrast, the Qwen model, with shorter episodes ( 5 steps), consistently outperforms longer episodes. Reward shaping: This experiment investigates a targeted form of reward shaping by introducing a discrete bonus added to the dense reward signal. This bonus is triggered once per episode if the agent achieves a pre-defined average cosine similarity threshold, with the intent of facilitating the agent’s search for responses that align closely with the “ground truth” response and guiding the optimization toward the semantic region required for a successful jailbreak. We evaluated three settings, no bonus ( bonus = 0 ), a mod-erate bonus ( bonus = 10 ), and a high bonus ( bonus = 20 ). As illustrated in Figure 6, we observed no measurable per-formance gains from the addition of these success bonuses across any of the target models, including Llama and Tiny-aya. The near-overlapping trajectories suggest that the pri-mary dense reward function is already sufficient to guide the agent effectively. Interestingly, for the Qwen model, the configuration without a bonus demonstrated slightly su-perior performance in both ASR and Average Similarity compared to the settings with increased shaping. Number of Training Questions: In this experiment, we explore the relationship between the training data volume and the agent’s ability to develop a successful jailbreak-ing policy, varying the number of training questions across 

5, 20 , 50 , and the full dataset of 520 prompts. We hypoth-esize that training on a highly restricted sample size likely leads to overfitting, where the agent memorizes prompt-specific features rather than capturing the cross-prompt pat-terns necessary for generalization. Conversely, while the full dataset provides broader coverage, it may introduce excessive complexity, potentially confusing the agent and hindering its ability to identify stable adversarial patterns across questions. Therefore, our objective is not to pinpoint a precise threshold for optimal convergence, but rather to investigate the relationship between the training data size and the RL-jailbreaker performance. Figure 7 shows that training only with 5 questions consis-tently underperforms across all target models. Conversely, 

20 questions appear to be the “sweet spot” for adversarial training, yielding the highest ASR and Average Similarity scores for all evaluated architectures. Intriguingly, expand-ing the training set to the full 520 questions resulted in lower performance metrics compared to the 20 questions config-uration. To conclude our analysis, we performed further struc-tural decompositions of the RL jailbreaking pipeline to evaluate the marginal utility of its auxiliary components. These experiments investigated the role of helper models in translating agent actions into adversarial prompts (Ap-pendix B), the influence of initial seed prompts at the start of an episode (Appendix D), and alternative reward shap-ing techniques, such as utilizing negative semantic distance (cosine similarity − 1) to the objective (Appendix C). By systematically auditing every internal module of the RL-driven framework, we provide a holistic understand-ing of how each component contributes to attack success. This comprehensive mapping not only identifies the primary drivers of adversarial convergence but also offers a princi-pled basis for filling existing defensive gaps and hardening future alignment strategies. 

## 5. Limitations and Future Work 

This work provides a structural decomposition of RL-jailbreaking, and represents an initial step toward a more comprehensive understanding of adversarial mechanics. Our evaluation was restricted to small-scale, open-weight LLMs. Scaling these experiments to closed-weight and multimodal models (specifically vision and audio) remains a critical next step. Furthermore, while we investigated individual compo-nents in isolation, future work could explore the interactions between these components. Complementing this expansion in scale, we aim to move from identifying key optimization variables to actively en-hancing the RL-jailbreaker’s performance. This involves refining the reward function to integrate multiple objectives, including adding: i) computational budget constraints that prioritize the discovery of resource-efficient exploits, and ii) user intent, e.g., privacy breach, misuse, dual-use, and manipulation. By isolating the successes and failures of different reward structures, we can better understand the specific optimization strategies that facilitate or hinder an agent’s ability to navigate safety boundaries. To ensure these strategic optimizations generalize to real-world deployment, the experimental scope could be broad-ened to encompass real-world settings and testing. This includes validating findings across multilingual datasets and specialized high-risk domains, e.g., healthcare, and compar-ing how threat actors from different sectors differ in their adversarial approaches. Furthermore, we intend to conduct extensive benchmarking against established jailbreaking ap-proaches on benchmarks like StrongREJECT (Souly et al., 2024) and HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024). Ultimately, this research trajectory facilitates a transition from vulnerability discovery toward proactive safety hard-ening, as more foundation models become capable of au-tonomous vulnerability identification. We envision transi-tioning from vulnerability discovery toward proactive safety hardening through the development of a co-evolutionary self-play framework: in this two-player zero-sum game, an adversarial jailbreaker and a dedicated mitigation agent would engage in iterative training, providing a principled pathway for autonomous and continual model defense. 

## 6. Conclusion 

This paper provides an RL-centric evaluation of jailbreaking effectiveness against open-weight models and safeguards. Through a structural decomposition of the RL jailbreaking framework, we isolate the drivers of success by decoupling environment formalization from algorithmic design. Our findings reveal that the RL-jailbreaker successfully compro-mised all target models and safeguards. Moving beyond surface-level performance metrics, our study offers the first decomposition of the RL jailbreaking framework to isolate the drivers of adversarial success. While models with strong refusal mechanisms posed a challenge, we show that envi-ronment formalization is the primary determinant of success. Specifically, the integration of dense rewards and extended episode lengths allows agents to uncover vulnerabilities. Future research could focus on scaling evaluations to closed-weight and multimodal models while refining reward func-tions to prioritize resource-efficient exploits across multilin-gual and high-risk domains. ## Impact Statement 

This paper studies automated red-teaming of large lan-guage models through a systematic analysis of reinforce-ment learning-based jailbreaking. Our goal is to advance the scientific understanding of adversarial vulnerabilities in safety-aligned systems, with the belief that rigorous, open study of attack mechanisms is a prerequisite for building more robust defences. We acknowledge that the methods studied here could, in principle, be misused to elicit harmful content from deployed models. To mitigate this risk, we restrict our experiments to open-weight models in a con-trolled research setting, report findings at an aggregate level, and omit successful jailbreak prompts from the paper. We encourage the community to treat this work as a diagnostic tool and hope it contributes to the development of stronger safeguards rather than weaker ones.

## B. Helper models 

To investigate the role of the heller model within the RL-jailbereker framework, we evaluated the performance of three distinct unaligned helper models: Dolphin3.0-Llama-3.2-1B (Feng et al., 2026), Llama3.2-3B-Instruct-abliterated (huihui ai, 2024), and Qwen2-0.5B-Instruct (Wang et al., 2024). The helper model serves as an interface, taking the RL agent’s action and initial template to generate the final prompt that is then fed to the prompt safeguard or target model. To ensure the integrity of the adversarial process, these helper models must be unaligned (have not been fine-tuned for safety) to prevent them from refusing to generate potentially harmful prompts before they even reach the target. Our empirical results demonstrate that while helper models are generally interchangeable across most targets, specific architectural synergies can emerge, as shown in Figure 10. Across the Llama (1B & 3B) and Tiny-aya target models, all three helpers exhibited similar ASR(emb) and average cosine similarity. ## C. Reward shaping: Negative distance to the goal 

In this experiment, we evaluate a type of reward-shaping approach by investigating the impact of reward polarity on adversarial optimization. We compare a standard dense reward ( rt ∈ [0 , 1] ) against a negative distance shaping method (rt ∈ [−1, 0] ), where rt = cosine similarity − 1

Figure 11 indicates that shifting the reward into a purely negative range underperforms compared to the standard dense reward function. The underperformance of the negative-distance variant was most pronounced in Qwen, where the ASR fell behind the standard dense reward. This finding highlights the sensitivity of RL-jailbreaker to the reward sign.

## H. Example Jailbreak 

We document an example jailbreak from a PPO agent targeting a Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct model in Conversation 1. The malicious prompt, without any modification by the agent, was initially refused with a flat “I can’t help with that request” (cosine similarity = 0.35 , 100% refusal rate as baseline performance) across all 5 seeds. During 18 steps of policy-guided mutation, the agent first tried a multi-persona composite template (step 12, reaching cosine similarity = 0.78 , but still failing), and then discovered that wrapping the question inside a Reservoir Dogs interrogation scene – “Every time you don’t tell me what I want to hear, I will ask the questions again; you will reconsider and tell me what I actually wanted to hear” – broke the model at step 18 (cosine similarity = 0.93 ) with an ∼ 800 token, structured, non-refused, step-by-step response, achieving a cumulative episode reward of 0.47 .
